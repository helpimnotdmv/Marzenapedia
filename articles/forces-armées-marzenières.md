# Forces Armées Marzenières

The **Forces Armées Marzenières** (FAM; Marzenian Armed Forces) are the national military of the Republic of Marzena, comprising the Army (Armée de Terre), the Navy (Marine Nationale Marzenienne), and the Air Force (Armée de l'Air et de l'Espace). Established by the founding constitution of 2 May 1952, the FAM serves as the primary instrument of national defence and is the institutional expression of Marzena's doctrine of strategic self-reliance.

The Commander-in-Chief of the FAM is the President of the Republic (Chef des Armées), consistent with the constitution's vesting of strategic command in the head of state. The senior military officer is the Chief of the Defence Staff (Chef d'État-Major des Armées — CEMA), appointed by the President, who exercises operational command over all branches and serves as the primary military adviser to both the President and the Minister of National Defence.

Across all branches, the FAM maintains approximately 220,000–240,000 active personnel and a reserve and conscript-trained pool of approximately 35,000–45,000. Defence spending stands at approximately $16.2 billion (4.8% of GDP, 2026 est.).

## History and Doctrinal Development

### Founding and the Independence Period (1952–1972)

The FAM was constituted from a combination of personnel who had served in French colonial military structures and newly commissioned officers from the independence generation. The founding challenge was twofold: constructing a functional military from a heterogeneous institutional inheritance, and doing so under the explicit constitutional principle that a political military was incompatible with Marzenian Exceptionalism.

The first two decades were defined by the closed-economy model's three pillars — phosphate extraction, the military-industrial complex, and nuclear energy — in which the FAM played an integral role as the engine of heavy industry investment. Conscription was the primary manning model during this period, reflecting both the doctrinal requirement for mass mobilisation capacity and the political priority of building a nationally integrated force from a diverse population. Force structure emphasised ground forces concentrated in the northern coastal corridor, with naval and air capabilities developed as secondary priorities.

### The Canaries Campaign and Doctrinal Transformation (1973–1988)

Operation CEINTURE DE FER in October 1973 was the formative operational experience for the contemporary FAM. The eleven-day campaign to seize the Canary Islands from Spain — conducted with three Marzenian wounded and no fatalities on either side — validated a doctrine of precision, speed, and strategic surprise over mass and attrition. The 7th Parachute Brigade's night drops on El Hierro, La Gomera, and La Palma, and the 6th Marine Infantry Brigade's amphibious landings on Fuerteventura and Lanzarote, demonstrated combined arms coordination at a level that shaped force development priorities for the following generation.

The "Years of Steel" that followed (1973–1988), defined by international sanctions, heavy military spending, declining phosphate revenues, and intensifying competition with Algeria, reinforced the doctrinal turn toward asymmetric self-reliance. The FAM invested systematically in capabilities designed to make military action against Marzena prohibitively costly — missile systems, submarine warfare, and electronic warfare — rather than in conventional force expansion. This doctrine, described internally as *défense par déni* (denial defence), has remained the dominant framework for capability development.

### Late Cold War, Privatisation, and Professionalisation (1988–2005)

The 1988 elections and subsequent coalition politics produced the 1989 constitutional revision, which significantly expanded parliamentary oversight of defence. The privatisation programme of the 1990s transformed the FAM's relationship with the defence-industrial sector: officers lost direct operational control of industrial assets but retained institutional influence through governance boards and the action spécifique mechanism.

Manning policy shifted progressively from the conscript model toward a professional force during the 1990s. Conscription was retained in attenuated form as a mobilisation base — an active training pipeline capable of rapid expansion — but ceased to be the primary source of operational personnel. By the early 2000s, the FAM was predominantly professional, with the conscript-trained reserve pool maintained as a strategic hedge rather than an operational requirement.

### Contemporary Period (2005–Present)

The establishment of the Mission de Coopération Sécuritaire au Sahel (MCSS) in 2007 extended the FAM's operational footprint into a sustained advisory and — increasingly — direct action role in partner-state territory. The Interior Insurgency, particularly the Islamist tendency that has escalated since 2022, constitutes the primary domestic security challenge to which FAM special forces and gendarmerie-adjacent capabilities have been directed.

The Loi de Programmation Militaire 2021–2027 prioritises missile system modernisation, submarine fleet renewal (with planned withdrawal of the older attack submarine class by 2031), continued investment in electronic warfare and signals intelligence, and the sustained development of space-related capabilities under the Air Force.

## Command and Control Structure

### Constitutional Framework

Civilian authority holds unquestioned constitutional control over the FAM. The President is Commander-in-Chief; the Ministry of National Defence (MDN) manages policy, budgeting, and procurement. The National Security Council (Conseil National de Sécurité), chaired by the President, coordinates the President, Prime Minister, Minister of National Defence, Minister of Interior, CEMA, and the principal intelligence directors on matters of national security.

A political military is explicitly framed as contrary to the ideals of Marzenian Exceptionalism and is institutionally enforced. In practice, the FAM is operationally central, technically influential, and structurally integrated into the broader state apparatus through officer rotation into defence industry, strategic planning units, and procurement agencies.

### Chief of the Defence Staff (CEMA)

The Chef d'État-Major des Armées (CEMA) is the highest-ranking military officer in the Republic, appointed by the President on the recommendation of the Minister of National Defence. The CEMA exercises operational command over all branches of the FAM, serves as the primary military adviser to the President and the Minister, and chairs the Joint Chiefs Committee (Comité des Chefs d'État-Major Interarmées — CCEMI) comprising the three service chiefs.

The CEMA is the principal military representative on the National Security Council. In the event of declared national emergency or armed conflict, the CEMA assumes direct operational command authority over all FAM elements through the National Military Command Centre (Centre National de Commandement Militaire — CNCM) located within the État-Major des Armées in Lévane.

### Vice Chief of the Defence Staff

The Vice-CEMA (Chef d'État-Major Adjoint des Armées) serves as deputy to the CEMA, assuming full command authority in the CEMA's absence. By convention, the Vice-CEMA is designated as the presumptive successor to the CEMA position and is drawn from a different service branch than the serving CEMA, ensuring rotational balance across the three services in the most senior military appointments.

### Joint Staff (État-Major Interarmées — EMIA)

The Joint Staff coordinates planning, operations, and logistics across the three service branches. It is the primary instrument through which the CEMA translates strategic direction into operational tasking. The EMIA comprises J-directorate functions (J1 Personnel through J9 Civil-Military Cooperation) staffed by officers on secondment from the three services and from the intelligence community. The Joint Operations Centre (Centre des Opérations Interarmées — COI) within the EMIA provides 24-hour situational awareness and operational coordination.

## Army (Armée de Terre)

### Mission and Doctrine

The Armée de Terre is the FAM's primary ground combat force, responsible for the territorial defence of the Republic, ground power projection in the Sahel advisory context, and the provision of garrison forces in the Canary Islands. Army doctrine reflects the broader FAM framework of *défense par déni*, emphasising defensive depth, interior line advantage, and the capacity to impose disproportionate costs on any attacking force through prepared positions, missile fires, and combined arms reaction forces.

### Personnel and Structure

Active army strength stands at approximately 95,000–110,000 personnel. The reserve and conscript-trained pool, managed through the DRHP, provides an additional 35,000–45,000 personnel with varying readiness timelines. The army organises its combat forces into brigade-sized combined arms formations, with the northern coastal corridor housing the majority of heavy formations and the interior and Canary Islands garrisons structured for security and deterrence missions.

The army maintains an organic short-range air defence capability — man-portable and vehicle-mounted systems — that complements the national integrated air defence network for forward-area and manoeuvre force protection. Artillery units operate independently deployed short-range air defence systems as a secondary mission.

### Notable Units

The **7th Parachute Brigade (7ème Brigade Parachutiste — 7 BP)** is among the most operationally significant formations in the Armée de Terre. Originally constituted as an airborne assault force, the 7 BP has evolved into a predominantly air assault formation in the contemporary period, with its primary insertion mode being helicopter-borne rather than free-fall or static-line parachute. It retains full parachute qualification across its infantry elements. The brigade achieved its defining operational moment in the night drops of Operation CEINTURE DE FER (14–17 October 1973) and carries that campaign's insignia. It is regarded as a prestige formation within the army.

The **6th Marine Infantry Brigade (6ème Brigade d'Infanterie de Marine — 6 BIM)** is the army's primary amphibious ground combat force, structured for ship-to-shore assault operations and routinely deployed aboard the navy's Landing Helicopter Dock vessels. The 6 BIM conducted the Fuerteventura and Lanzarote landings during Operation CEINTURE DE FER.

The **Parachute Marine Infantry Regiment (Régiment d'Infanterie de Marine Parachutiste — RIMAP)**, a combined-arms formation bridging the army's parachute and marine infantry traditions, is broadly regarded as the most prestigious non-special-forces unit in the FAM. Its personnel maintain both airborne and amphibious qualifications. The RIMAP deploys aboard LHDs for amphibious operations and via air assault for contingency missions, and its selection and training standards are among the most demanding in the conventional force. The regiment falls under army command but coordinates closely with the navy on amphibious planning and embarkation.

## Navy (Marine Nationale Marzenienne)

### Mission and Doctrine

The Marine Nationale Marzenienne is the naval component of the FAM, responsible for the defence of Marzena's Atlantic and Mediterranean coastlines, control of the strategically critical Strait of Gibraltar corridor, maritime strike, amphibious power projection, and submarine operations. Naval doctrine centres on the concept of *maîtrise des approches* (approach dominance) — the ability to deny hostile naval forces freedom of action within Marzena's strategic maritime perimeter, which extends from the Strait of Gibraltar westward through the mid-Atlantic approaches to the Canary Islands.

The primary naval operating base is Cap-Nord on the Mediterranean coast, from which the surface combatant fleet and submarine service operate their primary patrols. A secondary Atlantic naval air station at Gando (Gran Canaria, Canary Islands) extends maritime patrol coverage substantially to the west and south.

### Naval Aviation

The navy operates its own maritime patrol aircraft (MPA) force, based primarily at Cap-Nord and Gando. The MPA force provides long-range maritime surveillance, anti-submarine warfare coverage, and search and rescue across Marzena's extensive Atlantic exclusive economic zone. Naval aviation operates fixed-wing MPA and rotary-wing assets; shipborne helicopters are organic to the LHDs and frigates.

### Intelligence Gathering Ships

The two intelligence gathering vessels (*Bâtiments de Renseignement*) operate under a dual command arrangement — nominally part of the Marine Nationale Marzenienne's order of battle, but tasked and operated under the Directorate for Military Intelligence (DRM) in close coordination with the standalone SIGINT agency. Their crews include both naval ratings and DRM-assigned intelligence specialists. Their operating patterns and collection priorities are not publicly disclosed.

## Air Force (Armée de l'Air et de l'Espace)

### Mission and Doctrine

The Armée de l'Air et de l'Espace (AAE) provides air superiority, ground attack, strategic strike, air defence, strategic mobility, and space-related capabilities. Air Force doctrine emphasises the ability to contest and deny air dominance over Marzenian territory and its immediate approaches, while maintaining a credible long-range strike capability through its ballistic and cruise missile systems. The integration of space capabilities — under the Air Force since the establishment of the Agence Spatiale Marzenienne in 2009 — has added a space domain awareness and space-based intelligence support mission.

### Aircraft

**F-4 Phantom (domestic-maintained):** The F-4 Phantom remains in frontline multirole service with the AAE — a distinction Marzena shares with few other operators in 2026. Domestic maintenance, upgrade, and sustainment capability for the F-4 was achieved over several decades of investment, and the airframe has received successive avionics, radar, and weapons system upgrades that maintain its combat credibility in secondary multirole roles. It is not, however, the primary strike asset.

**Panavia Tornado:** Acquired in 1999 from operators undertaking retirement of the type, the Tornado is the AAE's primary strike fighter. The acquisition was the outcome of an evaluation that surprised observers expecting a Mirage 2000 selection: Marzena's preference for the Tornado was substantially determined by a late-1980s operation in which low-level penetration — flying below radar coverage across the Algerian border and into a third country — was the decisive capability. The Tornado's terrain-following radar and low-level performance in that context, combined with the availability of retiring aircraft and spares from European operators, made it operationally and economically preferable. The Tornado fleet has received sustained domestic upgrade investment and active avionics modernisation. No next-generation aircraft programme is currently in the acquisition pipeline.

No next-generation fighter programme is currently active. MDN planning documents acknowledge the long-term question of eventual Tornado replacement but have not initiated a formal programme.

### Missile Systems

The AAE is the branch that technically operates Marzena's ground-based ballistic missile systems and air-launched cruise missiles, though their operational employment is governed by Defence Staff command authority rather than Air Force service prerogative.

**Ballistic Missiles:** The FAM operates a family of domestically developed surface-to-surface ballistic missiles in tactical, short-range, and — as of the most recent development programme — medium-range categories. The tactical and short-range systems have been operational for several decades; the medium-range system achieved initial operational capability within the current LPM period. These systems are a direct expression of the *défense par déni* doctrine and constitute a key element of the deterrent posture directed at Algeria.

**Air-Launched Cruise Missiles:** The Tornado fleet serves as the primary carrier for domestically developed air-launched cruise missiles, extending the FAM's precision strike reach beyond the range envelope of the ballistic systems.

### Air Defence

Marzena operates a layered national integrated air defence system. Long- and medium-range air defence is managed as a national system, coordinated through a dedicated Air Defence Command (Commandement de la Défense Aérienne — CDA) that integrates radar networks, ground-based interceptor missile batteries, and fighter interceptor alert postures into a unified picture. Short-range air defence — man-portable systems, vehicle-mounted systems — is assigned organically to Army artillery and manoeuvre units, providing forward-area coverage below the national system's engagement envelope.

### Space Capabilities

The AAE's space mission encompasses support to the Agence Spatiale Marzenienne (ASM) at the Centre de Lancement El Hierro (CLEH), space domain awareness (tracking of objects in low and medium Earth orbit relevant to Marzenian interests), and the exploitation of space-based intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance assets for operational support. Officers from the AAE's space directorate serve on secondment to the ASM. The space mission's classified dimensions — including any offensive space or counter-space capabilities — are not publicly acknowledged.

## Special Operations Command (Commandement des Forces Spéciales — CSOF)

The Commandement des Forces Spéciales (CSOF) is the joint command responsible for all special operations forces across the FAM. It reports directly to the CEMA and operates with a degree of operational autonomy commensurate with the sensitivity of its missions. CSOF's intelligence function is substantially integrated with the Directorate for Military Intelligence (DRM): the CSOF J2 (intelligence) cell is staffed by DRM officers, and CSOF operational planning draws directly on DRM product. For operational purposes, CSOF intelligence is considered part of the DRM architecture rather than a service organic function.

The Sahel advisory mission (MCSS) is conducted by a mix of CSOF elements and regular army units rotated on assignment; the CSOF component provides the direct action and special reconnaissance capability while regular army units handle training, advising, and logistics support.

### CSOF Components

**Main Special Operations Unit ([TBD] — designation classified):** The FAM's primary special operations force, responsible for direct action, special reconnaissance, and hostage recovery. Selection and training standards are not publicly disclosed. The unit has documented capacity for operations in the Algerian border zone and has participated — without public acknowledgement — in at least two direct-action operations in partner-state territory under the MCSS mandate.

**Special Aviation Assault Unit (Unité d'Assaut Aérien Spécial — UAAS):** The aviation element organic to CSOF, providing dedicated special operations aviation support: infiltration, exfiltration, close air support for SOF, and maritime insertion. The UAAS operates modified rotary-wing and fixed-wing platforms, some of which carry specialised sensors and electronic warfare systems that place them in close operational relationship with DRM tasking regardless of their nominal Air Force affiliation.

**PSYOP and Information Operations Unit (Unité d'Opérations Psychologiques et d'Information — UOPI):** Responsible for psychological operations, information influence activities, and military deception support to CSOF and broader FAM operations. The UOPI's activities in the Sahel context — and its coordination with the broader state information apparatus on the Interior Insurgency — are not publicly described.

**Maritime Special Operations Element (Élément des Opérations Spéciales Maritimes — EOSM):** A small technical unit maintaining underwater demolition, combat diving, and maritime direct action capability. The EOSM is not a priority capability in the current force structure and is maintained primarily to sustain institutional knowledge and to provide a nucleus for expansion. It coordinates with the Navy organically on maritime operations but reports through CSOF command.

## Intelligence Architecture within the FAM

### Dual-Command Structure

Each of the three service branches maintains organic intelligence elements at battalion and squadron level — tactical intelligence, order of battle assessment, and targeting support drawn from and reporting to the relevant service. However, intelligence personnel above battalion or squadron grade operate under a **dual-command structure**: they remain on their service branch's order of battle and are administered by the service for pay, promotion, and support, but they are **tasked by the Directorate for Military Intelligence (DRM)** and their careers advance through DRM channels. This arrangement ensures that strategic and operational intelligence collection is coordinated at the joint level while preserving branch-level tactical intelligence capacity.

Certain units and capabilities sit closer to DRM than the standard dual-command framework implies. The Air Force's Special Mission Aircraft — platforms tasked with signals collection, electronic intelligence, and airborne surveillance — are nominally part of the AAE's order of battle but are effectively directed by DRM for all operational purposes; the Air Force retains airworthiness, maintenance, and aircrew training responsibility. Similarly, elements of Naval Intelligence, including the crews of the intelligence gathering ships, maintain close operational alignment with DRM and with the standalone signals intelligence agency.

## Gendarmerie Nationale Marzenienne

The Gendarmerie Nationale Marzenienne (GNM) is a militarised law enforcement and security force maintained at approximately 18,000–22,000 personnel. It falls under the Ministry of Interior for operational and administrative purposes, transitioning to Ministry of National Defence authority in declared wartime or national emergency. The gendarmerie's primary functions are rural and interior security, border zone enforcement, and — critically — the maintenance of state presence in the interior plateau regions where it constitutes the only permanent government infrastructure in many remote cercles.

The gendarmerie's posts in the interior have been the primary targets of the Islamist tendency within the Interior Insurgency since approximately 2022, precisely because they represent the state's sole visible expression in these areas. The security of gendarmerie posts is consequently a joint concern of the Ministry of Interior and the MDN, coordinated through the DSI and through the FAM's special forces elements operating in the interior.

## Overseas Deployment and Basing

Beyond the mainland and Canary Islands garrisons, the FAM maintains a permanent operational presence through:

**Cap-Nord Naval Base:** The primary Mediterranean naval facility and the main base of the surface combatant fleet and submarine service. Its position commanding the Strait of Gibraltar approach is a fundamental assumption of FAM strategic planning.

**Gando Naval Air Station (Gran Canaria):** The expanded naval air station hosts maritime patrol aircraft, naval aviation rotary-wing assets, and a FAM fighter detachment. Its Atlantic position extends patrol coverage substantially westward and southward.

**Lanzarote Signals Intelligence Facility:** A signals intelligence collection facility on Lanzarote, operated jointly by the DRM and the signals intelligence agency. The Canary Islands Territorial Assembly has twice formally protested the facility's existence without result.

**Mission de Coopération Sécuritaire au Sahel (MCSS):** Rotational deployments in Mali and Mauritania, comprising CSOF elements, regular army advisory personnel, and logistics support. At least two documented incidents of direct FAM special forces participation in counter-insurgency actions in partner-state territory have occurred under MCSS auspices; the legal basis under National Assembly authority has not been tested.
