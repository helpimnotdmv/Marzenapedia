# Interior Insurgency

The **Interior Insurgency** is the collective designation used in Marzenian security and policy discourse for a persistent low-intensity armed conflict affecting the central and southern interior plateau regions of the Republic of Marzena. It is not a unified movement and has never had a single command structure, political programme, or coordinated military strategy. Three distinct tendencies have been documented by the Direction de la Sécurité Intérieure (DSI) and acknowledged — in varying degrees of explicitness — in official communications: the **Mouvement de Résistance des Plateaux** (MRP), operating under a Berber cultural grievance framing; the **Réseau Sud**, an economically motivated network substantially merged with informal cross-border trade structures; and the **Islamist tendency**, formally organised as **Jaysh al-Watan al-Haqiqi** (JWH), which has become the primary focus of the DSI's counterterrorism directorate and the FAM's operational commitment in the interior since 2022.

The three tendencies are fragmented in organisation, motivation, and programme. They share geography and, in some sub-regions, a limited tactical accommodation — though the nature and extent of that accommodation is disputed in assessments available to the public. What they share most concretely is the state's sustained failure to develop the interior regions in which they operate, and the gendarmerie posts that represent, in many cercles, the only permanent expression of the republic's presence.

The government's public framing of the insurgency has evolved. For most of the period from the early 1980s through 2021, official communications characterised interior security incidents as criminal activity, banditry, or isolated extremism — language that avoided acknowledging a sustained armed challenge to state authority. The escalation of 2022 — marked by a coordinated JWH assault on a FAM forward patrol base that resulted in the first confirmed military fatalities from hostile action in the interior — forced a shift. Since late 2022, official statements have acknowledged "a serious security situation in the southern interior zones" and referenced "organised armed groups with external connections," without using the word insurgency. Parliamentary debate has been more direct.

## Background and Context

### The Interior as Political Geography

The central and southern interior plateau regions of Marzena — covering a substantial portion of the country's land area but a small fraction of its population and economic output — have been structurally marginalised since independence. The closed economic model of the founding period concentrated industrialisation along the northern coastal corridor, treating the interior principally as a labour reserve and a source of raw materials. The deliberate policy of drawing interior populations into coastal wage labour — described in internal government documents of the period as weakening the social cohesion of tribal structures — simultaneously disrupted traditional economies without replacing them with equivalent livelihoods for those who remained.

State presence in the interior takes forms that are primarily extractive and regulatory rather than developmental. Gendarmerie posts, tax collection offices, and the infrastructure serving phosphate extraction and power transmission are the most visible state institutions in many cercles. Healthcare, secondary education, and reliable road access are absent or severely limited across large sub-regions. The 2004 constitutional revision's devolution provisions have been implemented slowly and incompletely outside the northern corridor. The **Cadastre National Unifié**, mandated in 2004 to resolve the land registration pathology created by the renaming programme, remains 34% complete nationally — with coverage concentrated almost entirely in the north.

This context does not explain the insurgency in any deterministic sense — the great majority of the interior's population is not insurgent and does not support armed groups — but it is the environment in which all three tendencies have recruited, sustained themselves, and found at least passive shelter.

### The Renaming Programme and Its Legacies

The government's renaming programme of 1953–1975, which replaced traditional Arabic and Berber settlement names with French or invented Marzenian compound names, is the MRP's primary historical grievance and a live source of cultural tension across the interior more broadly. Of the 847 settlements formally renamed, only approximately 340 names achieved adoption in everyday usage; the remainder continue to be known by traditional designations, creating a persistent divergence between official cartography and lived geography. The legal pathology this produced — multiple naming conventions in successive generations of land records — remains administratively unresolved and is a recurring source of grievance in communities where land rights are disputed.

## The Mouvement de Résistance des Plateaux (MRP)

The MRP was established in 1981 in the central plateau region, emerging from the intersection of Berber cultural activism, land rights organising, and a generation of interior youth who had experienced the coastal industrial economy as culturally alienating rather than emancipatory. It is the oldest of the three tendencies and, in terms of organisational coherence and political programme, the most developed.

### Programme and Character

The MRP's stated objectives centre on three demands: the recognition of Berber languages in official use alongside French, including in education and public administration; the reversal of the renaming programme or at minimum its formal acknowledgement as a cultural imposition; and land restitution or compensation for interior communities displaced by state industrialisation and infrastructure development in the founding decades. These demands are cultural and civic in framing — the MRP consistently presents itself as a resistance movement seeking recognition within the republic rather than as a secessionist or revolutionary organisation.

The MRP's armed dimension has always been secondary to its political and cultural activities. In the 1980s and early 1990s, MRP-affiliated cells conducted acts of sabotage — communication infrastructure, phosphate transport routes, and, in two documented incidents, gendarmerie vehicles — that were calibrated to impose costs without producing mass casualties. Since the mid-1990s, the movement has substantially demilitarised at the organisational level, though cells in specific sub-regions retain the capacity for armed action and have used it sporadically in response to security force operations. The DSI classifies the MRP as an insurgent organisation; the MRP's external representatives, when they speak publicly — primarily through diaspora networks in France and through the legal political journal *La Voix du Plateau*, published in Fernant — describe it as a cultural resistance movement with a self-defence capacity.

The MRP explicitly rejects Gulf-state funding, a position that distinguishes it sharply from networks associated with the Islamist tendency and that it has maintained as a point of organisational identity. Its funding comes primarily from diaspora contributions and from the informal economy of the plateau communities in which it is embedded.

### Internal Tensions

The MRP is not internally uniform. An older leadership generation — predominantly in their 60s and 70s, some based in France — prioritises the political and cultural programme and is deeply reluctant to see the movement associated with violence or with the Islamist tendency's operations. A younger cohort, embedded in the plateau communities most directly affected by the security force response to JWH activity, has developed informal contact with elements of that network — primarily around shared knowledge of terrain and gendarmerie patterns. The older leadership is understood to be unaware of the full extent of these contacts, or to have chosen not to be informed of them. This internal fault line has not yet produced an organisational rupture, but it constitutes one of the DSI's more consequential current analytical uncertainties.

## Réseau Sud

The Réseau Sud is the DSI's designation for a loosely affiliated set of armed networks operating across the southern interior and desert approaches to the Mali and Mauritania frontiers. It is the least politically coherent of the three tendencies and the most accurately described as a protection economy rather than an insurgency in any conventional sense.

### Structure and Motivation

Réseau Sud networks are primarily organised around the taxation and protection of informal cross-border trade flows — the extended family and tribal commercial systems that account for an estimated 6–8% of Marzenian GDP and that predate the state by generations. Armed capacity is maintained to protect trade routes, resolve disputes with competing networks, and resist gendarmerie enforcement operations that threaten commercial operations. There is no unified political programme, and the Réseau Sud label is, in the DSI's own internal usage, a convenience for a set of phenomena that share geography and occasional coordination rather than a coherent organisation.

The cross-border dimension is structural rather than conspiratorial: the tribal trade networks that Réseau Sud-affiliated groups protect operate across the Marzenian, Malian, and Mauritanian border zones simultaneously, and the security forces of all three states face essentially the same problem from different angles. Algerian intelligence services have historically used the commercial networks of the southern border zone for low-level deniable operations — the introduction of forged documentation, the movement of small quantities of materials, and the cultivation of informants — a practice that the DSI monitors without being able to interdict consistently.

### Relationship with the Islamist Tendency

In specific sub-regions of the southern interior, Réseau Sud-affiliated networks and JWH cells have developed a working relationship based on mutual utility rather than ideological alignment. JWH requires logistical support — movement of personnel and materiel across the Mali frontier — that the trade networks are structurally positioned to provide. The networks receive, in return, a degree of protection from state pressure in zones where JWH has established effective presence. This transactional accommodation has been documented in DSI assessments and is assessed as the most operationally significant intersection between the three tendencies.

## Jaysh al-Watan al-Haqiqi (JWH)

**Jaysh al-Watan al-Haqiqi** — rendered in Marzenian security and press usage as *Jaysh al-Watan* or abbreviated JWH — is the Islamist armed organisation that has constituted the primary domestic security threat in Marzena since its elevation to that status in the DSI's 2024 threat assessment, the first time in the institution's history that a domestic actor has ranked above Algerian intelligence activity. The group emerged approximately 2008 as a distinct network and has undergone substantial organisational development since, including a factional differentiation that is now a defining feature of its structure.

### Origins and Development

JWH's origins lie in the convergence of two processes: the transnational Islamist network activity that spread across the Sahel following the destabilisation of Mali and Libya in the early 2010s, and a domestic radicalisation dynamic specific to Marzena's second generation of interior urban migrants — young men raised in Solane and Fernant, connected to interior communities through family but without secure footing in the coastal francophone economy that defines official Marzenian identity.

The group is affiliated with a broader Sahel Islamist network whose structure and external connections are known to the DSI and to partner intelligence services but are not publicly disclosed in detail. The affiliation is assessed as providing ideological material, training access, and cross-border logistics rather than direct command and control — JWH's operational decisions are understood to be locally made, within a framework of shared ideological orientation. The group does not claim affiliation publicly in its communiqués, which are issued in Arabic and French and make no reference to external organisations by name.

### Ideology and Objectives

JWH's public communications articulate an ideological programme that is simultaneously territorial and anti-exceptionalist. The territorial objective — most explicit in communiqués associated with the more doctrinally rigid of the group's two factions — is the establishment of an Islamic emirate across the southern interior plateau: not a national takeover, but the creation of a defined territory under Islamic governance, severed from the secular republican framework. The anti-exceptionalist dimension is the primary recruitment instrument: JWH frames Marzenian Exceptionalism as a colonial imposition that replaced one external identity project (French colonialism) with another (secular francophone republicanism), and positions Islam and Arabic cultural identity as the authentic foundation that the republic suppressed. This framing has demonstrated appeal specifically to the second-generation urban recruit profile — young men whose experience of the republic is one of partial inclusion and cultural dislocation.

### Factional Structure

JWH operates as a single insurgent organisation with two internally recognised tendencies that share military command and logistics but differ in ideological emphasis and operational culture.

The first, referred to in DSI materials as the **"doctrinal faction"**, prioritises the territorial emirate programme and adheres to a stricter interpretation of Islamic governance. It is more resistant to tactical compromise, more focused on controlling specific geographic areas, and more oriented toward the longer-term project of building parallel governance structures — rudimentary courts, taxation of local populations, enforcement of conduct codes — in zones where it has established effective presence.

The second, referred to as the **"resistance faction"**, foregrounds the anti-exceptionalist grievance and frames the group's violence explicitly as revenge for state suppression of the interior — the renaming programme, the displacement of communities by industrialisation, the decades of gendarmerie presence without commensurate services. It draws more heavily on the local interior population and is more willing to accommodate the practical interests of communities in which it operates. It is assessed as tactically more flexible and operationally more effective in the short term.

The two factions operate under a shared leadership structure whose composition is not publicly known. Communiqués are issued jointly; operational decisions in the conflict zone appear to be made by a local commander or commanders whose identity has not been publicly confirmed by the DSI. The factional distinction is an analytical construct derived from communiqué analysis and source reporting rather than a formal organisational division acknowledged by the group itself.

### Recruitment

Current DSI analysis assesses peer networks as the primary recruitment pathway, with online material providing ideological exposure and peer contact providing the operational bridge. The profile of the typical JWH recruit is consistent across available reporting: male, aged 19–28, with family origins in the interior plateau or southern regions, partial secondary or tertiary education in Solane or Fernant, employed in insecure or informal work or unemployed, and experiencing a sense of cultural dislocation that the francophone coastal identity offers no vocabulary to address. The ideology provides an identity framework that is neither the exceptionalist civic ideal — which these recruits experience as an exclusion — nor the traditional interior identity, which is associated with a world their parents left.

A smaller cohort of university-educated recruits has been identified in DSI assessments, following a distinct intellectual radicalisation pathway, but this profile remains a minority within the broader recruit population.

## The Active Conflict Zone

### Location and Geography

The most intense phase of the insurgency — characterised since late 2022 by sustained armed engagement between JWH and the Forces Armées Marzenières at a tempo and scale without precedent in the republic's interior security history — is concentrated in a mountainous sub-region of the central interior plateau. The area's name is the subject of the naming programme's legacy: official cartography uses a French-derived designation; local population uses a Berber toponym; FAM operational documents use a grid-reference designation. The sub-region is characterised by complex terrain — broken plateau, elevated ridgelines, a network of dry wadis that provide covered movement routes — minimal road infrastructure, sparse civilian population, and proximity to the Algerian border zone that complicates operational freedom of action.

The terrain favours the defender and the irregular: movement by conventional wheeled or even tracked vehicles is severely constrained outside the main tracks, helicopter operations are subject to elevation and weather limitations, and drone coverage has documented gaps in the deeper wadi systems during night operations. JWH has exploited these characteristics deliberately.

### The 2022 Threshold

The operational turning point that forced the government's shift in public framing was a coordinated JWH assault in October 2022 on a FAM forward patrol base in the conflict sub-region. The attack, conducted at night using indirect fire followed by a ground assault, resulted in seven FAM fatalities and fourteen wounded — the largest single loss of life in a domestic security incident in the republic's history. The assault demonstrated a level of planning, coordination, and weapons capability that the DSI's prior assessments had not anticipated, and that implicated external training input in the near-unanimous assessment of the intelligence community.

The attack was not publicly described in these terms at the time. Official communications referenced "a serious incident involving armed criminal elements" and announced reinforcement of interior security operations. The National Assembly's Defence Committee received a classified briefing; the existence of the briefing, though not its contents, was reported by Le Témoin. The shift in official language — from criminal framing to explicit acknowledgement of "organised armed groups" — occurred over the following months.

### Current Operational Picture

The conflict as of 2026 bears a closer resemblance to a genuine counter-insurgency campaign than to the low-intensity security operations that characterised the interior for the preceding four decades. JWH's assessed strength in the active conflict zone is 150–180 fighters, out of a wider network of approximately 200–250 across all roles. The discrepancy between the zone strength and the DSI's earlier estimates of total JWH numbers suggests external reinforcement — a finding consistent with the assessed Sahel network affiliation.

JWH's operational pattern encompasses ambushes of FAM patrols on mountain tracks, IED belts on the primary supply routes, mortar use against forward positions, and, in the conflict zone's more controlled areas, the rudimentary governance activities associated with the doctrinal faction — enforcement of conduct codes, rudimentary dispute resolution, and taxation of movement through JWH-held terrain. In several locations, fortified positions in cave systems and abandoned mining infrastructure have required deliberate clearance operations rather than the mobile counter-ambush response that the FAM's initial operational posture envisaged.

The FAM's committed force in the zone draws primarily on COI Sud: elements of the 4th and 5th Light Infantry Brigades, Army Aviation Command rotary-wing assets including attack helicopters, and MALE drone operations providing persistent surveillance and, in confirmed engagements, precision strike. A CSOF element provides direct action and special reconnaissance capability. A forward operating base has been established in the sub-region, currently in early operational stages and assessed as a commitment to a sustained rather than episodic campaign.

The FAM's operational difficulties are both terrain-related and human. The complex ground constrains the advantages of wheeled mobility and limits helicopter operational windows. JWH fighters' familiarity with the terrain, and their demonstrated ability to disappear into the civilian population of the sparse surrounding villages when pressure increases, has reduced the effectiveness of clearance operations. A major clearance operation conducted in mid-2024 succeeded in temporarily displacing JWH from two fortified positions but did not destroy the group's capacity in the zone; JWH reconstituted its presence in one of the two positions within three months. This outcome is understood in defence and security circles, though it has not been publicly reported in detail. The ideological commitment of the second-generation urban recruit cohort has also been assessed as higher than anticipated — these fighters have not shown the susceptibility to economic incentives or negotiated accommodation that has occasionally been effective with Réseau Sud-affiliated networks.

## Political Dimensions

### Parliamentary Debate

Parliamentary discussion of the insurgency has expanded since 2022 but remains constrained by the classification of operational details and the government's reluctance to use language that implies a sustained armed challenge to state authority. The **MSM** has pressed most consistently on the developmental and governance failures underlying the insurgency, arguing that security-first operations without commensurate investment in interior services are compounding the conditions that generate recruitment. The **MNP** supports maximum military commitment. The **PNLR** government has defended its approach as calibrated and proportionate.

The **Alliance Nationale Conservatrice** occupies an uncomfortable position. The party has officially and publicly condemned JWH's violence in unambiguous terms — the civic current's condemnation is genuine and consistent, and the party has cooperated with parliamentary oversight mechanisms on the security response. The traditionalist current has, on several occasions, made public statements that, while not endorsing JWH, attributed the insurgency primarily to the state's suppression of Islamic expression — formulations that the DSI monitors and that have generated internal ANC tension. The traditionalist current's Gulf-associated funding networks occupy a proximity to the early-stage radicalisation networks that the DSI's counterterrorism directorate tracks without characterising as operational connection. The ANC's civic current is aware of this proximity and regards it as the most politically damaging aspect of the insurgency's continued escalation.

### The Developmental Question

Within the government, a reform tendency — concentrated in the Ministère de l'Intérieur's civilian policy directorate and represented by at least one senior PNLR figure — has argued that the security-first approach to the insurgency, whatever its short-term operational logic, is accelerating recruitment by confirming the JWH narrative of a state that responds to the interior with force rather than with services. The security establishment — the DSI, COI Sud, and the relevant MDN directorates — has consistently contested this framing, arguing that development without security is not achievable in the conflict zone and that JWH's ideological character makes it unresponsive to developmental incentives in the way that Réseau Sud-affiliated networks sometimes are.

The announced interior infrastructure programmes of 2021 and 2023 — road extensions, healthcare facility construction, and an accelerated Cadastre completion schedule for affected arrondissements — are the visible output of this internal debate. Implementation has been partial. In the conflict zone specifically, infrastructure construction has been suspended pending security stabilisation, creating a circular problem that neither the security nor the development faction has resolved.
