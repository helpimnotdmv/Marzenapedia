# Ministère de la Défense Nationale

The **Ministère de la Défense Nationale** (MDN; Ministry of National Defence) is the cabinet-level ministry of the Republic of Marzena responsible for defence policy, military administration, armaments procurement, and the political oversight of the Forces Armées Marzenières (FAM). Established by decree on 2 May 1952 — the day of independence — the MDN is the principal governmental institution through which civilian authority is exercised over the armed forces.

The ministry is headed by the Minister of National Defence (Ministre de la Défense Nationale), a political appointee who reports jointly to the President of the Republic and the Prime Minister consistent with Marzena's semi-presidential constitution. The ministry is distinct from the military command hierarchy: operational authority over the FAM is vested in the Chief of the Defence Staff (Chef d'État-Major des Armées — CEMA), who is accountable to the President as Commander-in-Chief but works within the policy and budgetary framework set by the MDN.

The MDN's permanent headquarters is the Palais de la Défense, a purpose-built complex on the western edge of Lévane's civic axis, completed in 1961.

## History

### Founding Period (1952–1964)

The ministry was created simultaneously with independence as part of the Constitutional Commission's architecture for civilian control of the new republic's armed forces. The founding generation of Marzenian nationalists, drawn from a francophone professional and legal class, was instinctively hostile to the prospect of military political power. The 1952 constitution accordingly placed the armed forces under a civilian ministry reporting to both the executive presidency and the legislature — a dual accountability mechanism that remained a persistent source of institutional tension through the first decade of the republic.

The early MDN was a lean administrative apparatus. The FAM itself, in its immediate post-independence form, comprised units and personnel inherited from the colonial military structure and was considerably smaller than subsequent decades would produce. Defence policy in this period was substantially dictated by the security dynamics of the Arab League confrontation (1955–1965) and the closed economic model that channelled state resources into the military-industrial sector.

### Constitutional Revision and Expansion (1964–1975)

The 1964 constitutional revision that entrenched secularism also introduced new mechanisms of central fiscal control, several of which directly affected MDN authority. A classified annex to the defence appropriation — the first of what became a structural feature of Marzenian defence budgeting — was introduced in 1966 to fund the nuclear programme's dual-use infrastructure. The ministry expanded its directorate structure during this period to manage the growing complexity of the state-directed defence-industrial sector.

The Canaries Campaign of October 1973 (Operation CEINTURE DE FER) was planned and authorised at the presidential and National Security Council level with minimal MDN bureaucratic involvement — a pattern consistent with the campaign's emphasis on speed and secrecy. The ministry's post-campaign role was primarily administrative: managing the new garrison requirements, integrating the Canary Islands into the defence estate, and absorbing the institutional lessons of the operation.

### Privatisation Era and the Action Spécifique (1990s)

The partial privatisation programme of the early-to-mid 1990s was the most structurally consequential transformation of the MDN's relationship with the defence-industrial sector. Significant portions of the sector, previously under direct state ownership, were transferred to mixed public-private structures. The political mechanism that made this acceptable to the FAM — and that the MDN administered — was the *action spécifique*: a golden share held by the state in all privatised defence entities, granting veto rights over ownership changes, major strategic decisions, and technology transfer. The Directorate for Defence Industry and Dual-Use Technologies (DIDTD) was established in 1994 in part to manage this new governance architecture.

In 2012, the MDN formally designated dual-use technology conversion as a named policy enabler of the privatisation programme — the **Programme d'Intégration des Technologies à Double Usage (PITDU)**. The programme argued that economies of scale, reduced duplication, faster R&D cycles, and spin-in/spin-off benefits between the defence and civilian industrial base justified structural integration of defence and commercial technology development. The PITDU became the institutional framework within which the Directorate for Defence Industry and Dual-Use Technologies operates.

## Political Leadership

### Minister of National Defence

The Minister of National Defence is the political head of the MDN and the primary interface between the armed forces and elected government. The minister is appointed by the President on the recommendation of the Prime Minister. In conditions of cohabitation, the minister's relationship with the presidency becomes more complex: defence and foreign policy remain within the presidential domain under the constitution, but budgetary and procurement decisions require parliamentary approval and thus prime ministerial backing.

The minister chairs the Ministerial Defence Council (Conseil Ministériel de Défense), a standing body that coordinates policy between the MDN directorates and the FAM service chiefs. The minister represents Marzena in international defence forums and is the political authority responsible for the Loi de Programmation Militaire (LPM), the five-to-seven year defence planning law that governs defence expenditure.

### Secretary of State for Defence

The Secretary of State for Defence (Secrétaire d'État à la Défense) is a junior ministerial position that exists at the discretion of the government of the day — it has not been included in every MDN composition since independence. When appointed, the Secretary of State typically holds a delegated portfolio, historically covering personnel affairs, veteran policy, or specific industrial programmes. The position has been more frequently filled during periods of coalition government, where it has served as a mechanism for distributing ministerial posts among coalition partners.

### Director-General of the Ministry of Defence

The Director-General (Directeur Général du Ministère de la Défense) is the most senior civil servant in the MDN, responsible for the ministry's internal administration, interministerial coordination, and the translation of political directives into bureaucratic and budgetary action. Unlike political appointees, the Director-General is a career official drawn from the senior civil service. The position carries significant institutional continuity across changes of government and is a major node in the relationship between the elected minister and the permanent MDN apparatus.

---

## Inspectorate-General of the Armed Forces

The Inspectorate-General of the Armed Forces (Inspection Générale des Armées — IGA) is an independent audit and inspection body that reports directly to the Minister of National Defence. Its mandate covers the operational readiness, administrative compliance, and financial propriety of all FAM components, the MDN's own directorates, and OCPM. The IGA's direct line to the minister — rather than to the Director-General — is a deliberate constitutional choice, intended to give the body teeth in circumstances where a finding implicates the ministry's own senior civil service or military command.

IGA inspection reports are classified by default, with summary findings transmitted to the National Assembly's Defence Committee on a discretionary basis. Opposition parties have repeatedly pressed for mandatory reporting; the practice remains at ministerial discretion.

## Directorate Structure

The MDN administers its functions through nine principal directorates. Each directorate is headed by a Director (Directeur), a senior civil servant or, in the case of military-adjacent directorates, a senior officer on secondment.

### Directorate for Defence Policy and Strategy (DPSD)

The DPSD is the ministry's long-term planning and doctrinal body. Its core output is the *Livre Blanc de la Défense* (Defence White Paper), the foundational document of Marzenian defence policy published on an approximately eight-year cycle, most recently in 2022. The DPSD coordinates with the Joint Staff on operational doctrine, with the intelligence community on threat assessment, and with the Directorate for International Relations on alliance and partnership obligations. Its policy planning horizon extends to fifteen years for capability development and thirty years for strategic environmental assessment.

### Directorate for Budget and Financial Affairs (DBAF)

The DBAF manages the MDN's total appropriation, currently approximately $16.2 billion annually (2026 est.), representing 4.8% of GDP — among the higher proportions in the region. The DBAF is responsible for the allocation of funds across the ministry's directorates and the FAM service budgets, financial oversight of classified programmes, and coordination with the Ministry of Finance on the annual budget process.

A structural feature of Marzenian defence budgeting is the classified annex (annexe classifiée) to the annual defence appropriation, a practice dating to 1966 that funds programmes whose existence or cost is not publicly disclosed. The classified annex currently covers OCPM's core research programmes, elements of the intelligence architecture, and selected capability development lines within the FAM. The DBAF administers the classified annex; oversight is exercised by the President, the Minister, and a restricted subcommittee of the National Assembly's Defence Committee.

### Directorate for Procurement and Armaments (DAA)

The DAA is the central procurement authority for major defence systems, responsible for managing acquisition programmes from requirements definition through contract award and in-service support. The DAA coordinates closely with OCPM on domestically developed systems and with the Directorate for International Relations on foreign procurement. For major programmes, the DAA chairs an interdepartmental Programme Board that includes representatives from the relevant service, OCPM, and DBAF.

The DAA's relationship with the privatised defence companies — Groupe SOMAR, CNAM, and ESDM — is governed by the action spécifique framework. All major contracts with these entities are subject to DAA approval, and the DAA holds the state's administrative seat on the golden share governance boards.

### Directorate for Defence Industry and Dual-Use Technologies (DIDTD)

Established in 1994 and significantly expanded following the 2012 PITDU designation, the DIDTD manages the state's relationship with the privatised and semi-privatised defence-industrial base. Its principal functions are the governance of the action spécifique mechanism across Groupe SOMAR, CNAM, and ESDM; the promotion and administration of the Programme d'Intégration des Technologies à Double Usage (PITDU); the oversight of technology transfer policy; and the export control regime administered jointly with the Commission de Contrôle des Exportations de Défense (CCED).

The PITDU, as administered by the DIDTD, operates through a system of joint technology development agreements between defence entities and commercial partners in aerospace, electronics, energy systems, and maritime technologies. Participation is voluntary but is incentivised through access to MDN-funded research infrastructure, including certain non-classified facilities adjacent to the Centre National d'Évaluation Technique.

### Directorate for Personnel and Human Resources (DRHP)

The DRHP administers military and civilian personnel policy across the MDN and FAM, covering recruitment standards, career management frameworks, training policy, pay and conditions, and veteran affairs. The directorate oversees the transition of the FAM from the mixed conscript model of the late Cold War to the predominantly professional force of the contemporary period, managing the residual conscript-training capacity that is maintained for rapid mobilisation contingencies.

The DRHP operates the Registre National du Personnel Militaire, the central record-keeping system for all serving and former military personnel. Veteran affairs — pensions, disability assessments, rehabilitation services — are administered through a subordinate agency, the Service National des Anciens Combattants (SNAC).

### Directorate for Infrastructure and Real Estate (DIRE)

The DIRE manages the entirety of the defence estate: military bases, port facilities at Cap-Nord and Solane, air stations, training ranges, and the support infrastructure associated with strategic facilities. This remit includes administrative coordination with the Agence Spatiale Marzenienne regarding the Centre de Lancement El Hierro on El Hierro, which the DIRE supports in its physical plant and security perimeter functions.

The DIRE maintains a classified inventory of the defence estate that encompasses facilities not acknowledged in public documentation, including the support infrastructure serving the Centre National d'Évaluation Technique. Proposed new construction projects — whether bases, roads serving military facilities, or port expansions — are assessed jointly by the DIRE and the FAM's logistics directorate.

### Directorate for International Relations and Cooperation (DIRC)

The DIRC manages Marzena's military diplomacy: attaché postings, bilateral defence cooperation agreements, and the oversight of the Mission de Coopération Sécuritaire au Sahel (MCSS). The directorate coordinates with the Ministry of Foreign Affairs on defence dimensions of bilateral relationships — most significantly the *Accord de Coopération Logistique* with France, renewed on a five-year rolling basis — and manages the defence export relationships with sub-Saharan partner states.

Defence exports to sub-Saharan partners are conducted under the restrictive transfer regime of the Commission de Contrôle des Exportations de Défense (CCED), for which the DIRC provides the policy and diplomatic framework while CCED provides the technical licensing function.

### Directorate for Military Intelligence (DRM)

The Directorate for Military Intelligence is the MDN's defence-specific intelligence body and one of the principal nodes in Marzena's broader intelligence community. It is described in detail in the article on the Marzenian Intelligence Community. Within the MDN structure, the DRM Director holds a seat on the National Security Council's intelligence subcommittee and maintains a direct reporting line to both the Minister of Defence and the CEMA.

The DRM's relationship with the FAM branches operates through a dual-command structure for intelligence personnel above battalion/squadron grade: such personnel remain on their branch's order of battle but are tasked and career-managed by DRM. Certain units — Air Force special mission aircraft, elements of Naval Intelligence — maintain closer operational alignment with DRM than the standard dual-command framework implies.

### Directorate for Legal Affairs and Litigation (DAJ)

The DAJ provides legal counsel across the MDN's functions: operational law, procurement law, international humanitarian law, and litigation management. It advises the minister and the Director-General on the legal dimensions of policy decisions and reviews all major contracts and international agreements before signature. The DAJ also manages Marzena's legal compliance obligations under the NPT framework and provides the ministry's interface with the IAEA on safeguards-related matters.

## Office for Capability Programmes and Materiel (OCPM)

The Office pour les Programmes de Capacité et le Matériel (OCPM) is the MDN's semi-autonomous defence research and development authority, responsible for leading weapons programmes from concept through prototype demonstration. Technically under the MDN, OCPM operates with considerable organisational independence: it maintains its own scientific and engineering staff, manages long-duration research programmes across budget cycles, and functions as the primary institutional interface between the FAM's capability requirements and the defence-industrial base.

OCPM was established in the early 1960s as the administrative home of Marzena's nuclear programme infrastructure. As the nuclear programme evolved and the state's defence-industrial ambitions expanded, OCPM's mandate broadened to encompass missile systems, naval platform systems, electronic warfare, and aerospace development. Following the privatisation programme of the 1990s, OCPM became the principal mechanism through which the MDN retained sovereign capability in the areas of design authority and systems integration — functions that the state was unwilling to fully transfer to the privatised industrial entities.

OCPM is the primary institutional user of the Centre National d'Évaluation Technique (see below). Its budget is funded through the classified annex of the defence appropriation and is not publicly disclosed. The OCPM Director-General holds the rank of senior civil servant equivalent and is appointed by the Minister on the recommendation of the Director-General of the MDN. By convention, OCPM leadership has been drawn from engineering and scientific backgrounds; several former directors have subsequently served as senior figures in the Agence Spatiale Marzenienne.

## Centre National d'Évaluation Technique

The **Centre National d'Évaluation Technique** (CNET) is Marzena's principal defence research, testing, and evaluation complex — a restricted government facility operating under the MDN through OCPM. It is the largest and most sensitive facility in the national defence research architecture.

The CNET is located in the interior plateau region, in an area of minimal civilian population, within a formally designated National Security Exclusion Zone (Zone d'Exclusion de Sécurité Nationale). The exclusion zone has been physically enforced since the facility's establishment in the early 1960s, when it was created to support the early nuclear programme's engineering and testing requirements. It was formally registered under this legal designation in the late 2010s — primarily as an administrative regularisation, after the logistical burden of maintaining an informal exclusion through distributed gendarmerie presence became impractical relative to a formal zoning instrument. Restricted airspace corresponding to the exclusion zone has appeared on civil aviation charts for decades, identified only by coordinates.

The facility was not publicly acknowledged by the MDN until the late 2010s. The proximate cause was not a political decision to disclose but an administrative one: formal registration of the exclusion zone required acknowledging the facility's existence in a public legal instrument. Rumours of the complex had circulated for many years prior, significantly amplified by the circulation, from the early 2000s, of a patch associated with the facility. The patch bears a Latin motto commonly rendered as *Dissolvendo Mensuram Veritatis* — "Dissolving the Measure of Truth." The origin of the phrase is genuinely obscure; it predates the current organisational structure of the facility and is not attributed to any known individual in available records. Within the relevant communities, the facility is frequently referred to as **DMV** — derived from this patch — rather than by its official designation.

OCPM is the CNET's primary operator and principal user. The facility's known functions include: missile systems development, propulsion testing, and range instrumentation; electronic warfare system testing and countermeasures development; aerospace research and prototype evaluation; and a general proving ground function for systems across the development portfolio. The facility maintains instrumented range corridors extending into uninhabited territory to the south. A second coastal maritime test range, administered by OCPM under a separate restricted zone designation, supports naval systems testing — particularly submarine acoustics, sonar, and torpedo development — from a facility on the southern Atlantic coast.

## Budget and Resources

The MDN's total annual appropriation stands at approximately $16.2 billion for 2026, representing 4.8% of GDP. This figure is among the higher defence-spending proportions in the region and reflects Marzena's sustained doctrinal commitment to defence investment dating from the closed-economy period of the 1950s–1970s.

The appropriation is divided between a public budget (published in the annual finance law and subject to National Assembly scrutiny) and a classified annex covering programmes whose existence or cost is treated as a state secret. The size of the classified annex relative to the total appropriation is not disclosed.

The Loi de Programmation Militaire (LPM), most recently covering 2021–2027, governs the multi-year allocation of defence resources across capability areas. The current LPM prioritises missile system modernisation, submarine fleet renewal (the planned withdrawal of the older attack submarine class by 2031 and its replacement), the fourth nuclear reactor at Kermane, and continued investment in electronic warfare and signals intelligence infrastructure.
