# Police Nationale Marzenienne

The **Police Nationale Marzenienne** (PNM) is the principal civilian law enforcement body of the Republic of Marzena, responsible for policing urban agglomerations and designated commune zones across the republic's mainland territory and the Canary Islands autonomous territory. It operates under the authority of the **Ministère de l'Intérieur et de la Sécurité Publique** and is commanded by the **Directeur Général de la Police Nationale** (DGPN), a civil servant appointed by the Council of Ministers on ministerial nomination.

The PNM is formally distinguished from the [[Gendarmerie Nationale Marzenienne]] (GNM), which exercises police powers outside urban zones — in rural, interior, and border areas. The division of territorial jurisdiction between the two services is set by decree and administered by an inter-ministerial commission; in practice, it is a persistent source of bureaucratic rivalry that has generated its own institutional literature and occasional parliamentary attention. In operational terms, the PNM is the face of the state in Marzena's coastal cities; the GNM is the face of the state in the interior cercles where the republic's presence is otherwise thin.

The PNM employs approximately 38,000 officers and civilian staff across all grades. Its largest operational commands are in Solane (approximately 11,000 officers for a metropolitan area of 3.8 million) and Lévane (approximately 5,500 for a capital of 1.5 million). The institution's history, culture, and internal politics have been shaped by three factors that distinguish it from comparable European services: the coastal/interior political geography of the republic, the sustained insurgency in the southern interior zones that has produced specific counter-terrorism pressures on urban policing, and the exceptionalist civic ideology that PNM officers internalise — and that gives their institutional self-image a character more ideological than that of most comparable police forces.

## History

### Colonial Origins and Independence Reconstitution

The PNM's institutional lineage runs through the colonial **Sûreté Générale**, the French-administered civil security service that policed the northern coastal cities under the colonial administration. The Sûreté was staffed at supervisory levels almost entirely by French nationals; Marzenian recruits occupied the lower uniformed grades in the colonial hierarchy and received systematically inferior training, equipment, and career prospects.

At independence, the Constitutional Commission's interior affairs subcommittee confronted a straightforward institutional problem: Marzena required a functioning police service from day one of the republic, but the service it was inheriting was colonial in personnel, culture, and structure. The solution was pragmatic and imperfect. The Sûreté was formally dissolved on 2 May 1952 and immediately reconstituted as the Police Nationale Marzenienne under emergency decree; French technical advisers remained in senior supervisory positions on secondment until the early 1960s, while an emergency accelerated training programme — conducted partly in France, partly at a hastily established academy in the outskirts of Lévane — produced the first cohort of Marzenian-trained supervisory officers.

The accelerated training programme left its marks. Officers promoted quickly on the basis of political reliability rather than professional formation, formal procedures that existed on paper but had not been institutionalised in practice, and a set of informal habits — shortcuts in documentation, the exercise of discretionary authority without record, the management of informant networks outside formal intelligence structures — that colonial policing had embedded and that the transition did not displace. The 1971 reorganisation was the first serious institutional attempt to rationalise these inheritance problems; it produced the three-directorate structure that, in modified form, still governs the PNM today.

### The 1971 Reorganisation

The **Loi organique de 1971 portant réorganisation de la Police Nationale** replaced the Sûreté's inherited divisional structure with a modern departmental architecture. The three principal directorates — the **Direction Centrale de la Sécurité Publique** (DCSP), the **Direction Centrale de la Police Judiciaire** (DCPJ), and the **Direction Centrale des Renseignements Généraux** (DCRG) — were established as formally distinct commands reporting to the Directeur Général. The 1971 law also created the **Inspection Générale des Services** (IGS) as an independent oversight body charged with investigating officer misconduct, a structure whose powers on paper have consistently exceeded its practical effectiveness.

The 1971 reorganisation took place against the background of significant social upheaval in the coastal cities: rapid urbanisation, a first wave of interior-to-coast migration producing dense informal settlement on the outskirts of Solane and Fernant, and the first significant emergence of organised criminal networks in the port city. The DCPJ's creation was directly motivated by the need for a specialised criminal investigation capacity that the uniformed Sécurité Publique was not equipped to provide.

### The 1989 Reforms and Their Consequences

The constitutional revision of 1989 — which substantially expanded parliamentary powers and introduced new oversight mechanisms across the executive branch — also mandated a review of police powers and civil liberties frameworks. The resulting **Loi de 1989 relative aux libertés publiques et à l'organisation policière** introduced formal constraints on pre-charge detention, codified interrogation rights, and significantly expanded the IGS's nominal powers. It also — in a provision whose significance was not immediately apparent — consolidated the **Direction Centrale du Renseignement Intérieur** (DCRI) by merging the DCRG's political intelligence function with elements of the [[Direction de la Sécurité Intérieure]] (DSI)'s urban operational capacity. This merger, intended to rationalise the overlap between domestic intelligence and police intelligence, produced an institutional boundary dispute between the PNM and the DSI that has never been definitively resolved and that is a source of active jurisdictional friction in counterterrorism matters.

## Structure

### Direction Centrale de la Sécurité Publique (DCSP)

The DCSP is the PNM's uniformed public order and patrol branch — the largest of the three directorates in headcount and the most visible in everyday life. It encompasses the urban commissariats and their patrol officers (*gardiens de la paix*), the traffic police, and the uniformed first-response capacity of the major cities. DCSP officers staff the police posts that represent the PNM's primary street-level presence.

The DCSP is also responsible for the **Compagnies de Maintien de l'Ordre** (CMO), the public order units that manage civil disturbances, demonstrations, and large crowd events. The CMO units are trained to French CRS standards but have developed practices adapted to the specific character of Marzenian urban unrest — in particular, the management of demonstrations in interior-migrant community areas of Solane and Fernant, which carry cultural and political dimensions that standard crowd-management doctrine does not fully address. The deployment of CMO units into interior-migrant neighbourhoods during periods of social tension has been the subject of repeated parliamentary questions and several IGS investigations whose conclusions were not made public in full.

### Direction Centrale de la Police Judiciaire (DCPJ)

The DCPJ is the PNM's criminal investigation branch — its detectives, investigators, and forensic specialists. It is the most operationally prestigious directorate and the most institutionally opaque. DCPJ officers are plainclothes; they do not patrol but are assigned to cases, brigades, and operations. Entry to the DCPJ requires a minimum of three years in uniformed service and a competitive selection process; in practice, the selection is as much social as formal, with brigade commanders exercising significant informal influence over who is admitted and on what terms.

The DCPJ is organised into specialised brigades covering organised crime, financial crime, narcotics, homicide, counter-terrorism, and—in a division whose jurisdictional relationship with the DSI is a recurring institutional problem—political and security offences. The largest single DCPJ command is the **Brigade Criminelle de Solane** (BCS), which handles the port city's homicide and serious violent crime caseload and has a permanent establishment of approximately 420 investigators and support staff.

The DCPJ's most significant and most disputed unit is the **Section de Contre-Terrorisme et des Affaires Spéciales** (SCTAS), established in 2009 as a joint DCPJ–DSI operational cell and based formally within the DCPJ's counter-terrorism directorate. The SCTAS investigates the urban dimensions of the interior insurgency — primarily JWH recruitment networks, financing flows, and the movement of second-generation urban migrants toward the conflict zone — and is the primary interface between the PNM and the DSI. Its formal structure is DCPJ; its operational practice, funding, and internal reporting are partly DSI. Officers seconded to the SCTAS are understood by their colleagues to have "crossed over" in some sense that is institutional rather than personal; their subsequent career trajectories within the DCPJ are complicated by the association.

**The Badge and the Brigade.** A practice that originated in the DCPJ's organised crime brigades in the 1980s and spread throughout the directorate is the wearing of the police identification badge on a neck chain rather than in a wallet or on a belt clip — the standard method for uniformed officers. The neck badge is not a formal regulation and is not endorsed in any PNM documentation; it emerged organically as a social marker distinguishing the plainclothes investigator from the uniformed patrol officer, a visible claim to a different professional identity. It has become, within the DCPJ, an item of institutional meaning whose significance is understood by those who wear it and by those who observe them. Senior DCPJ officers wear it as a matter of course; officers new to the directorate begin wearing it as an act of identification with the brigade they have joined. The IGS has no opinion on the badge's mode of wear.

### Direction Centrale du Renseignement Intérieur (DCRI)

The DCRI is the PNM's intelligence directorate, responsible for monitoring urban-domain threats, political surveillance in the domestic security sphere, and the maintenance of registered informant networks across the major cities. Its relationship with the DSI is governed by a classified Memorandum of Understanding that defines information-sharing protocols and operational boundaries; in practice, the boundary is contested in specific cases and the MOU has been renegotiated twice since its initial signature in 1993.

The DCRI maintains officers in every major city commissariat and has liaison relationships with the GNM's intelligence component. Its focus areas include the surveillance of ANC-adjacent networks in coastal cities, monitoring of Arabic-language community media and associational life, and the tracking of individuals identified as potential JWH recruitment targets or facilitators. The DCRI's methods — informant cultivation, communications monitoring, physical surveillance — are subject to formal judicial authorisation requirements under the 1989 reform law; the extent to which those requirements are followed in practice has been a recurring subject of IGS investigation and, less frequently, of judicial proceedings.

### Compagnies Républicaines de Sécurité (CRS)

The PNM's reserve mobile force — the **Compagnies Républicaines de Sécurité** (CRS) — comprises twelve companies of approximately 150 officers each, based in the major urban centres and available for rapid deployment across the republic at the DGPN's discretion. The CRS companies are trained in crowd management, riot response, and area reinforcement; they are not assigned to local commissariats but held centrally under DCSP operational command.

The CRS deployment record over the past decade includes multiple deployments in the Solane port district during labour disputes, several interventions in the interior-migrant neighbourhood outskirts of Fernant following community incidents, and at least two deployments in the Canary Islands — one for a large PIC political demonstration in Santa Cruz, one for a civil disturbance in Las Palmas with no direct political character. CRS deployments are reported to the Interior Minister; they are not subject to prior parliamentary approval.

### Groupe d'Intervention Rapide (GIR)

The **Groupe d'Intervention Rapide** (GIR) is the PNM's tactical intervention unit — the SWAT equivalent, specialising in hostage rescue, armed suspect apprehension, and high-risk building clearance. It maintains a primary unit in Solane (approximately 90 operators) and a secondary capability in Lévane (approximately 60 operators). A smaller GIR element is based at the Gando naval air station in the Canary Islands for Atlantic territory requirements.

The GIR operates under a command structure that is nominally DCPJ but has direct-access protocols to the Interior Minister and, in terrorism-designated incidents, to the National Security Council. Its relationship with the FAM's **Commandement des Forces Spéciales** (CSOF) — the military special operations command — is documented in a classified operations coordination protocol governing scenarios where both capabilities might be deployed simultaneously.

## The Culture of the Corps

### *La Fraternité Bleue*: Solidarity and Its Consequences

The PNM's most significant and least officially acknowledged institutional feature is its internal solidarity culture — referred to within the corps as *la fraternité bleue* (the blue brotherhood) or, in older usage, simply *le code*. The culture is not unique to Marzena — comparable phenomena are documented in police services throughout the francophone world — but in Marzena it carries a particular ideological inflection that distinguishes it from the merely tribal: PNM officers frequently describe themselves not as enforcing the law in the abstract but as defending the republic against the forces that would dissolve it. This framing — in which the officer is a bulwark of Marzenian Exceptionalism, the secular republican order made flesh on the street — gives the solidarity culture an almost missionary character. To act against a colleague is, in this register, to weaken the institution that stands between the republic and its enemies.

In practical terms, *la fraternité bleue* operates through several mechanisms. Officers under IGS investigation routinely receive collective support from their brigade — character attestations, the slow-walking of documentary cooperation, the careful management of witness testimony. Officers who cooperate with IGS investigations find their career advancement disrupted by decisions — transfers, assignment changes, scheduling — that are formally discretionary and effectively impossible to challenge. IGS investigators who are former DCPJ officers are regarded by their former colleagues as having committed an institutional betrayal; their social relationships within the corps typically do not survive the appointment. Officers who bring allegations against colleagues through external channels — the judicial system, the press, the parliamentary ombudsman's office — face a version of the same reception at intensified magnitude.

The DCPJ's brigade structure intensifies these dynamics. A brigade is a working unit of eight to twenty officers who investigate together, share intelligence, cover each other's shifts, and in many cases socialise together outside working hours. The social pressure toward collective protection of a brigade member facing misconduct allegations is qualitatively different from the diffuse institutional solidarity of the larger service; it operates at the level of personal loyalty rather than institutional identity. IGS investigations into DCPJ brigades have historically produced the least cooperative environments in the corps.

The 2023 Le Témoin Marzenien series *"La Police et ses silences"*, which documented three separate cases where DCPJ officers facing serious misconduct allegations were protected by brigade solidarity and IGS investigations were stalled, was the most sustained public examination of *la fraternité bleue* in recent years. The series generated an Interior Ministry statement affirming the IGS's independence and announcing a review of investigative procedures; the review's findings have not been published.

### The Exceptionalist Officer

The PNM recruits disproportionately from two populations: the working-class families of the northern industrial corridor, and the first and second generation of interior-to-coast migrants who have undergone francophone assimilation in Solane and Fernant. The second population is, in the DCPJ's culture, particularly significant. Officers from interior migrant families who have succeeded in the francophone coastal institutional world through the PNM's career structure are often, in the observation of IGS social researchers and parliamentary staff with access to corps demographics, the most committed adherents of the exceptionalist civic identity — precisely because they have built their professional and personal lives on it and because the alternative — the communal, Arabic-speaking, religiously inflected identity they left or partially left — is institutionally proximate to the subjects of their investigative work.

This dynamic produces a particular cultural character in the DCPJ's brigades dealing with interior-migrant community crime, JWH-adjacent recruitment cases, and ANC-adjacent networks: officers who understand the communities they are investigating intimately, who have navigated the same cultural distance their subjects are navigating, and who have resolved that navigation in a direction that inclines them toward institutional rather than communal loyalty. The IGS and the parliamentary Commission des Lois have both noted, in documents not made public in full, that this population of officers produces the most effective investigators in community-embedded cases and the most difficult disciplinary subjects when those cases produce allegations of disproportionate force or procedural irregularity.

### The Interior Posting and Its Culture

PNM jurisdiction extends to urban agglomerations, but several northern interior towns of sufficient size fall within the PNM's designated commune zones rather than GNM jurisdiction — a bureaucratic determination that places some of the republic's most socially complex communities under urban police rather than gendarmerie authority. Postings to these interior-zone commissariats are the least sought in the corps; they combine the social complexity of interior-migrant communities with the resource constraints of a secondary priority budget line and the professional isolation of being far from the career-advancing brigades of Solane and Lévane.

Officers posted to interior-zone commissariats for extended periods — typically because they have been moved from preferred postings following misconduct findings or because they lack the social connections to secure transfers — develop a culture that blends the PNM's institutional identity with the GNM's frontier-enforcement mentality: isolated, under-resourced, and operating in communities where the line between the informal economy, the religious network, and the insurgent-adjacent structure is genuinely difficult to identify. The SCTAS has drawn a disproportionate number of its informant cultivation successes from officers with extended interior posting experience; it has also drawn a disproportionate number of its internal allegations.

## Oversight and Accountability

### Inspection Générale des Services (IGS)

The **Inspection Générale des Services** is the PNM's internal oversight body, established by the 1971 reorganisation and substantially empowered — on paper — by the 1989 reforms. It has an independent directorate, a formal mandate to investigate officer misconduct including criminal allegations, and the authority to refer cases to the judicial system where evidence warrants. Its investigators have IGS identification and, formally, cannot be directed by PNM brigade commanders.

In practice, the IGS's effectiveness is structurally limited. Its workforce is drawn from the same officer corps it investigates; while formal firewalls exist between current and former assignments, the social networks of the corps are not firewalled, and IGS investigators are known individuals within a closed professional world. The directorate is chronically understaffed: with approximately 340 investigators covering a corps of 38,000, the ratio of oversight capacity to policed population is among the lower ratios for comparable national police services in the francophone world. Case backlogs in the IGS average 18 months; in DCPJ brigade cases, the average extends to 26 months, a discrepancy the IGS's own annual reports attribute to the complexity of the investigations and that critics attribute to the complexity of the institutional resistance.

The 2024 IGS annual report — the most detailed released in the institution's history, published following sustained parliamentary pressure — documented 847 complaints received in the calendar year, 214 investigations opened, 61 disciplinary proceedings initiated, and 18 referrals to the judicial system. Twelve officers received sanctions ranging from formal reprimand to dismissal. The report's authors noted that complaint data almost certainly represents significant under-reporting, particularly in interior-migrant communities where trust in the complaints process is low and where the practical consequences of making a complaint against an investigating officer are understood to be significant.

### Parliamentary Oversight

The Assemblée Nationale's **Commission des Lois** (Laws Committee) exercises parliamentary oversight of the PNM through the annual budget review, questioning of the DGPN, and periodic thematic inquiries. The commission's capacity to scrutinise operational matters is limited by the classification of significant operational documentation and by the Interior Ministry's consistent position that operational methods are an executive rather than a parliamentary prerogative. The most significant parliamentary interventions on PNM conduct in recent years have been MSM-led questions on the DCRI's informant methodology in Arabic-speaking community associations, and ANC-led questions — carrying an evident complexity, given the ANC's own position under DSI monitoring — on the proportionality of CMO deployments in interior-migrant neighbourhoods.

## The PNM in the Canary Islands

The PNM operates a dedicated territorial command in the Canary Islands, the **Commandement Territorial de la Police Nationale aux Canaries** (CTPNC), with commissariats in Santa Cruz de Tenerife, Las Palmas, and the larger island towns. The Canarian command faces a specific set of challenges that distinguish it from mainland operations: a tourist economy that generates specific crime patterns (property crime, narcotics tourism, organised vehicle crime networks operating across Spanish and Marzenian legal jurisdictions), a Spanish-speaking majority that negotiates with French-speaking officers through an interpreter in formal proceedings, and a political environment — dominated by the CCM and the PIC — that generates occasional community tension without reaching the severity of mainland interior-region dynamics.

The PNM's relationship with the Canarian island administration is formally cooperative and informally uneasy. The Territorial Assembly has, on three occasions, passed non-binding resolutions calling for elements of policing devolution to a territorial law enforcement body that would recruit locally and operate in Spanish. The Interior Ministry has not responded to these resolutions in substantive terms; the CTPNC command treats them as a political matter outside its competence. The practical gap between a French-administered police force and a Spanish-speaking civilian population is managed through a bilingual officer recruitment target that has been partially but not consistently met.

## Relationship with the Gendarmerie Nationale Marzenienne

The formal division of territorial jurisdiction between the PNM and the [[Gendarmerie Nationale Marzenienne]] is administered by an **Inter-Ministerial Commission on Police and Gendarmerie Jurisdiction**, which reviews communal designations on a rolling basis and arbitrates disputes. The commission's existence is a reliable indicator of how persistent the disputes are: the PNM and GNM have been arguing about jurisdiction at the edges of expanding urban agglomerations since the 1971 reorganisation, and the commission meets quarterly to process a caseload that has not diminished.

The institutional rivalry between the two services is cultural as well as bureaucratic. PNM officers regard the GNM as a paramilitary body better suited to the interior's frontier environment than to professional civilian policing; GNM officers regard the PNM as an urban-oriented institution whose effectiveness does not survive the conditions of the interior. There is mutual professional respect at the level of specific operations and specific individuals; there is also mutual institutional contempt that is reproduced in training academies, career planning, and the dinner tables of officers' associations.

In counter-terrorism and insurgency-adjacent cases, the practical working relationship between the PNM's SCTAS and the GNM's intelligence and special operations elements — the **Section de Renseignement Opérationnel** (SRO) and the **Groupe d'Intervention de la Gendarmerie Nationale** (GIGN-M) — is operationally functional and personally fraught. Officers who have worked joint SCTAS–GNM operations describe the interoperability as adequate and the institutional sociology as exhausting.

## Relationship with the Direction de la Sécurité Intérieure (DSI)

The PNM's relationship with the [[Direction de la Sécurité Intérieure]] — the republic's domestic intelligence service, operating under presidential and prime ministerial authority rather than under the Interior Ministry — is the most consequential and most institutionally problematic of its inter-agency relationships. The DSI's urban operational capacity brings it into direct competition with the DCPJ's SCTAS for jurisdictional authority over the same cases: JWH urban networks, ANC-adjacent financing, and the movement of individuals between the coastal cities and the conflict zone. The formal MOU governing information-sharing between the DCPJ and the DSI resolves this competition in principle; in practice, it delineates an operational boundary that both services periodically cross without acknowledgement.

The specific tension is over intelligence cases that carry criminal dimensions — situations in which the DSI has developed intelligence on a network whose members could be prosecuted, but where a criminal prosecution would require disclosing the intelligence method. The DSI's institutional preference, in such cases, is to neutralise the network through administrative means — travel restrictions, asset monitoring, informant disruption — rather than through criminal process. The DCPJ's institutional preference is prosecution, which generates record, career advancement, and institutional visibility that administrative neutralisation does not. The conflict between these preferences has produced at least two documented cases — referenced in parliamentary committee minutes without details — in which DSI operations and DCPJ investigations ran simultaneously against the same target without full mutual awareness.
