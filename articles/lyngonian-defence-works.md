# Administration Pénitentiaire Marzenienne

The **Administration Pénitentiaire Marzenienne** (APM) is the state agency responsible for the operation of penal establishments, the detention of remanded and sentenced individuals, and the administration of non-custodial sentences across the Republic of Marzena. It operates under the authority of the **Ministère de la Justice et des Droits Civiques** and is directed by the **Directeur de l'Administration Pénitentiaire** (DAP), a senior civil servant appointed by the Council of Ministers.

The APM maintains 31 establishments across the mainland and the Canary Islands autonomous territory, ranging from urban remand facilities to maximum-security penitentiaries. As of 2026, the prison population stands at approximately 41,200 — a figure that includes approximately 3,400 individuals held under the **Centre d'Internement Sécuritaire** (CIS) regime, an administrative detention framework established by presidential decree in 2023 that operates partly outside the standard criminal process and whose legal status remains subject to active judicial and parliamentary contest.

The APM describes its mission as *sûreté, réinsertion, République*: the secure detention of those committed to its custody; the rehabilitation and reintegration of sentenced individuals into civic life; and the affirmation of republican values within its establishments. Critics — including the independent **Observatoire Pénitentiaire Marzenien** (OPM) and a consistent minority of parliamentarians across the MSM and ANC — argue that the second and third objectives have been systematically sacrificed to the first, and that the CIS framework represents an existential challenge to the third.

## History

### Colonial Inheritance

The APM inherited from the colonial administration a network of penal establishments concentrated in the northern coastal cities and designed primarily for the detention of colonial subjects — a population whose relationship to the law was defined by a separate legal code, administered with fewer procedural protections than the French citizens the colonial system was primarily designed to serve. The establishments themselves — several of which remain in use, substantially modified, in the contemporary APM network — were built to a standard that reflected the colonial administration's expenditure priorities: durable, imposing, and offering minimal consideration for inmate welfare.

The independence government's first justice minister announced in 1953 a programme of penitentiary reform that would transform the inherited colonial network into a modern republican corrections system consistent with the new republic's civic principles. The programme's ambitions were not matched by its resources. Several colonial-era establishments were renovated; one was demolished; the rest were recategorised within a new formal typology without significant structural modification. The legal framework was overhauled — the colonial two-track legal system replaced with a unified code of criminal procedure applicable to all citizens — but the physical infrastructure, the staff culture, and the informal practices of the inherited establishment persisted into the republican period substantially intact.

### The 2003 Penitentiary Law

The **Loi pénitentiaire No. 1847** of 2003 was the APM's most significant legislative reorganisation since independence. Passed following a series of OPM reports documenting systematic overcrowding, inadequate healthcare, and documented cases of guard violence in several major establishments, the law introduced minimum square-metre standards for individual accommodation, mandatory healthcare access requirements, a formal grievance procedure for inmates, and an independent inspectorate — the **Contrôle Général des Établissements Pénitentiaires** (CGEP) — with authority to inspect establishments unannounced and publish findings.

The 2003 law's implementation has been partial and contested. Overcrowding standards have not been consistently met in the urban maisons d'arrêt, where remand populations fluctuate with arrest rates and where the minimum accommodation standard is systematically exceeded during periods of high intake. Healthcare access requirements are met in larger establishments with dedicated medical units but remain inadequate in smaller facilities. The CGEP has conducted 847 establishment inspections since 2003, published 831 reports, and issued 214 formal recommendations; the APM's compliance with those recommendations is documented at 61% in the CGEP's most recent consolidated assessment. The 2022 Le Témoin series *"Derrière les murs"* — which documented conditions in four establishments using testimony from former inmates, recently released staff, and medical personnel — noted that the 2003 law's most significant practical achievement was the creation of a formal record against which departures from its standards could be measured, rather than the elimination of those departures.

## Facility Types

### Maisons d'Arrêt (MA)

The **Maisons d'Arrêt** are the APM's largest category by number and most visible by urban location: 16 establishments situated in or adjacent to the major cities, serving as both remand facilities (for individuals awaiting trial or sentencing) and short-sentence establishments (for sentences of two years or less). The urban maisons d'arrêt are chronically overcrowded. The **Maison d'Arrêt de la Cité-Port, Solane** — the republic's largest single penal establishment, with a designed capacity of 1,800 and a current population exceeding 2,600 — is the most documented example of this condition; it has been the subject of five separate CGEP formal recommendations on accommodation standards, none of which has been fully implemented.

The population profile of the maisons d'arrêt reflects the character of the cities they serve. In Solane, the Cité-Port population is disproportionately drawn from the port district's working class and interior-migrant communities, with significant representation from Arabic-speaking families and a notable contingent of individuals arrested in connection with the informal port economy — narcotics transit, counterfeit goods, unlicensed document services. In Lévane, the **Maison d'Arrêt de Karsène** serves a more mixed urban intake, including a category of white-collar and political-adjacent cases that the Cité-Port does not typically receive.

### Centres de Détention (CD)

The **Centres de Détention** are the APM's medium-security establishments, housing sentenced individuals serving terms of two to fifteen years in conditions designed to accommodate longer-term management and, nominally, preparation for eventual reintegration. Six CDs operate on the mainland; one smaller establishment in Gran Canaria serves the Canary Islands autonomous territory. The CDs maintain vocational training programmes, educational provision, and work programmes — the last of these accounting for a significant proportion of inmates' daily structure and, in the larger CDs, contributing non-trivially to the maintenance of APM facilities through labour supplied at internal accounting rates.

The CD at **Kermane** — the republic's third-largest penal establishment and the one most remote from major urban centres — has the most distinctive population profile: a significant proportion of its sentenced inmates are individuals convicted of insurgency-adjacent offences in the interior and southern regions, representing a geographic and cultural displacement from their communities that the establishment's reintegration programming is not specifically designed to address. The Kermane CD's distance from Solane and Fernant — where the majority of interior-migrant families of insurgency-convicted individuals now reside — also means that family contact visits, formally a right under the 2003 law, are in practice substantially curtailed by the cost and difficulty of travel for families in irregular economic circumstances.

### Établissements Pénitentiaires de Haute Sécurité (EPHS)

The APM operates two **Établissements Pénitentiaires de Haute Sécurité**: the **EPHS de Solane-Nord** and the **EPHS de Cap-Gris**, located respectively on the outskirts of Solane and in the northern coastal hinterland. Together they hold approximately 480 inmates under the highest security regime in the APM estate.

EPHS inmates are those classified as posing the highest risk of escape, the highest risk of violence within the establishment, or the greatest threat to national security if their detention is disrupted. In practice, the EPHS population comprises organised crime figures convicted of major sentences, convicted terrorists (domestic and international), and individuals whose cases carry intelligence significance that makes standard CD placement operationally inadvisable. The two EPHS establishments maintain dedicated intelligence liaison officers from the DSI and DCPJ — a formal arrangement under the 2003 law's intelligence cooperation provisions — whose presence and activities are not subject to the standard CGEP inspection regime.

The staff culture within the EPHS is the most intensified expression of the APM's broader corps culture. Officers serving long rotations in maximum-security environments — particularly those assigned to the high-security wings housing convicted JWH members — develop professional identities shaped by sustained proximity to individuals they regard, and who regard themselves, as committed enemies of the republic. The **Association des Personnels Pénitentiaires de Haute Sécurité** (APPHS), a professional association specific to EPHS officers, has been among the most vocal institutional voices calling for the expansion of administrative detention powers and the most consistent opponent of OPM and CGEP inspection access to high-security wings.

### Centres d'Internement Sécuritaire (CIS)

The **Centres d'Internement Sécuritaire** are the most controversial element of the APM estate, created by presidential decree in March 2023 in response to the escalation of the interior insurgency following the October 2022 attack on the FAM forward patrol base. Three CIS facilities currently operate: one within the EPHS de Solane-Nord complex, one in a converted military facility in the Kermane region, and one in a formerly civilian detention annex near the Algerian border zone.

The CIS framework authorises the administrative detention — without criminal charge or trial — of individuals assessed by the DSI as posing an imminent threat to national security in connection with the interior insurgency. Detention under the CIS regime requires a ministerial authorisation signed by the Interior Minister, renewable in six-month increments, and is nominally subject to judicial review by the **Chambre de Contrôle des Internements** (CCI), a specialised chamber of the administrative courts established by the same 2023 decree. The CCI's review process is conducted on the basis of classified intelligence summaries; detainees are represented by lawyers with security clearance drawn from a restricted panel; hearings are not public; and CCI decisions are not published, even in redacted form.

As of 2026, approximately 3,400 individuals are held under CIS authorisation. The OPM has been denied access to CIS facilities; CGEP inspectors have conducted one visit to the Solane-Nord CIS annex under constrained conditions and have not published findings. The 2024 parliamentary debate on the CIS framework — initiated by an MSM emergency resolution and supported by an unusual joint MSM–ANC–MNP majority — produced the closest thing to a public accounting of the regime: the Interior Minister confirmed the detainee total, described the CCI review process as robust, and declined to discuss conditions, individual cases, or the criteria applied in DSI threat assessments. The resolution passed without binding effect.

The CIS framework's constitutionality is the subject of an application before the Constitutional Court filed jointly by the Ligue Marzenienne des Droits Civiques and a panel of criminal defence lawyers. The Court accepted the application in late 2024; its proceedings are ongoing. The government has submitted a preliminary memorandum arguing that the CIS framework is consistent with Article 19 of the constitution — which permits the suspension of certain civil procedural protections in conditions of declared national emergency — and that the interior insurgency constitutes a sustained national emergency justifying administrative detention. The applicants contest both the factual basis of the emergency characterisation and the constitutional validity of the mechanism through which the 2023 decree sought to operationalise it.

## Centres de Réinsertion Civique (CRC)

The **Centres de Réinsertion Civique** (CRC) are a category of specialised establishment for which Marzena has no direct parallel in comparable national systems and which represents the most distinctly Marzenian element of its corrections architecture. Established under a 2009 Interior Ministry circular and subsequently incorporated into the APM's formal typology by the 2012 revision of the penitentiary law, the CRCs operate as custodial but non-penal facilities for individuals assessed as being at early stages of radicalisation toward JWH ideology or toward the Islamist tendency of the interior insurgency — typically young men in their late teens or early twenties, arrested in connection with JWH recruitment activity, possession of extremist material, or travel to the conflict zone without completion of a violent act that would support a terrorism charge.

The CRC programme combines custodial detention with an intensive educational curriculum designed around republican civic values: French language instruction at advanced levels, Marzenian constitutional history and republican civic theory, secular ethics, and vocational training oriented toward formal-sector employment. Participants are assessed at regular intervals by a panel including a psychologist, a CRC education officer, and — since 2019 — a DSI liaison analyst whose input concerns the intelligence dimension of the individual's network affiliations rather than their educational progress. Completion of the CRC programme nominally terminates the individual's file with the DSI counterterrorism directorate; in practice, CRC graduates are understood to remain on monitoring registers for a period of five years following release.

The CRC programme serves approximately 340 individuals at any given time across four facilities. Its stated success rate — measured by the absence of terrorism-related rearrest within five years of release — is quoted by the APM and the Interior Ministry as 78%. The OPM, in a 2023 report, queried the validity of this metric on several grounds: that absence of rearrest is not equivalent to successful reintegration; that the five-year monitoring period means that re-radicalisation after monitoring cessation is not captured; and that the intensive civics curriculum, while nominally educational, constitutes compelled participation in ideological programming in a manner that raises questions under Article 11 of the constitution — the guarantee of freedom of conscience. The APM's response described the OPM's characterisation as a misreading of an educational programme voluntarily entered by participants; critics note that the alternative to CRC participation for eligible individuals is standard terrorism-charge prosecution and EPHS detention, which is not a context in which the voluntariness of enrolment is readily assessed.

## The Death Penalty

The Republic of Marzena retains the death penalty in its Code pénal for three categories of offence: high treason committed in conditions of war or foreign occupation; terrorism resulting in the death of two or more individuals, where premeditation is proven; and the murder of a state officer (police, gendarmerie, judicial, or military) in the execution of their duties, where it is shown to have been carried out for the purpose of undermining state authority. The retention of the death penalty for these categories has survived multiple abolition bills — the most recent defeated by a narrow PNLR–MNP majority in 2019 — on the consistent government argument that its removal in the context of an active insurgency would send an operationally inadvisable signal about the state's willingness to impose terminal consequences on those who kill its officers.

In practice, the death penalty is applied with a frequency that reflects a political calculation as much as a legal one. Eleven executions have been carried out since independence, seven of them between 1972 and 1991, and the most recent in 2017 — the execution of a JWH-affiliated individual convicted of the bombing of a gendarmerie post in 2014 that killed four officers, following an appeal process that exhausted eight years of court time. Executions are carried out by lethal injection at the EPHS de Cap-Gris, which maintains the relevant infrastructure; the method was modernised from the guillotine — which the colonial administration had used and which was retained through the early republic — by a 1987 ministerial decree that framed the change as a matter of human dignity rather than of opposition to capital punishment itself.

Death sentences are imposed by the **Cour Criminelle Spéciale** (CCS), the court of jurisdiction for terrorism and treason offences, subject to automatic appeal to the **Cour de Cassation** and subsequent appeal opportunity to the **Conseil Constitutionnel**. The process, including all appeals, typically requires between six and twelve years from sentence to execution. The result is a population on death row — currently seven individuals, all convicted of terrorism-related murders — who occupy a legal status that combines the formal gravity of a capital sentence with the practical condition of indefinite detention while appeals proceed. The government has not pursued acceleration of the appeals process; it has also not, in current conditions, pursued a moratorium.

## Prison Population: Demographics and Dynamics

The APM's prison population of approximately 41,200 (including CIS detainees) is significantly overrepresented by several demographic groups relative to their share of the general population. Interior-region and Arabic-speaking individuals account for an estimated 38% of the sentenced prison population against approximately 24% of the general population. First and second-generation interior-to-coast migrants account for a further 21%. The Canary Islands-origin population is present at rates broadly proportionate to their share of the territory's total population. The overrepresentation of interior and migrant populations has been documented in OPM reports since 2007 without producing a policy response specifically addressed to the pattern; government responses have attributed it to socioeconomic factors and to the geographic concentration of insurgency-related convictions.

### The Bloc System

Informal social organisation within APM establishments — particularly in the larger maisons d'arrêt and the CDs — follows a pattern the APM formally neither acknowledges nor endorses but that its own operational experience has structured its internal management around. Inmates organise, with varying degrees of formality and durability, into *blocs* — social groupings defined primarily by regional origin, language community, and, in JWH-connected populations, ideological affiliation. The dominant blocs in most establishments are: coastal Marzenian (francophone, from the northern corridor); interior-migrant Arabic-speaking (typically from southern and central plateau families, speaking Moroccan-adjacent Arabic varieties); Berber-identity (smaller, associated with MRP-adjacent networks in some establishments and with no specific affiliation in others); and JWH-affiliated (ideologically distinct from the non-politically affiliated Arabic-speaking bloc, and typically kept physically separated from the general population in high-security wings).

The bloc system provides social support and protection within the establishment; it also provides the organisational structure through which contraband moves, grievances are coordinated, and, in JWH-connected cases, ideological material is disseminated within the institution. APM intelligence — managed by the **Renseignement Pénitentiaire** (RP), the APM's internal intelligence function — monitors bloc leadership, communication patterns, and the movement of material within establishments. The RP's relationship with the DSI provides a conduit for intelligence on JWH network activity within the prison population that is understood to flow in both directions: the DSI receives prison intelligence relevant to its external operations; the APM receives DSI assessments of individuals arriving in custody that shape internal classification decisions.

## Staff Culture

### The Corps Pénitentiaire and *La Famille des Murs*

APM surveillance officers — the *surveillants*, the basic grade of uniformed penitentiary officer — are recruited primarily from the same working-class and interior-migrant populations that constitute the bulk of the institutions' inmates, a demographic proximity whose irony is noted in academic literature on the Marzenian penal system and not acknowledged in official APM communications. The training academy — the **École Nationale de l'Administration Pénitentiaire** (ENAP) in Lévane — produces approximately 800 new officers per year, on a training programme of eighteen weeks that critics consistently describe as insufficient for the complexity of the environments new officers enter.

The staff culture that develops within APM establishments — particularly in the maisons d'arrêt and EPHS, where working conditions are most difficult — is characterised by the same internal solidarity dynamics that define the PNM's *fraternité bleue*, adapted to a residential rather than operational environment: officers work extended rotations, share the sustained stress of a confined institutional environment, and develop social ties within the corps that are qualitatively different from those in more episodic professions. The informal name for this culture — *la famille des murs* (the family of the walls) — reflects its character accurately: an intensely enclosed social world in which loyalty to colleagues is a survival mechanism and in which external oversight is experienced as an intrusion by those who do not understand the daily realities of the work.

Misconduct allegations within the APM follow patterns similar to those in the PNM: a formal complaints mechanism (the **Service de l'Inspection des Services Pénitentiaires**, SISP) that is understaffed, slow, and subject to the social pressures of a closed professional environment. The SISP received 621 formal complaints in 2024; it opened 189 investigations; it referred 14 cases to the judicial system. OPM researchers who have reviewed complaint patterns estimate that the submitted complaints represent between 15% and 25% of actual incidents — a figure the APM disputes without providing an alternative estimate.

### The EPHS Officer

Officers serving in the EPHS establishments occupy the highest-prestige posting in the APM career structure and, simultaneously, the most psychologically demanding. The sustained daily management of individuals convicted of mass-casualty terrorist attacks — in an environment where the ideological character of those individuals means that the officer–inmate relationship does not progress toward the instrumental accommodations that develop in non-ideological long-term detention — produces career-long occupational effects that the APM's employee wellness programme, established in 2018, has begun to document without yet developing effective responses to.

EPHS officers are also those most directly exposed to JWH's active influence operations within the prison: the recruitment of younger non-JWH-affiliated inmates by convicted JWH members, conducted through the ideological framework that has proven effective in the exterior insurgency context, represents a long-term threat to the APM that the RP and DSI monitor but that the physical design and staffing ratios of the EPHS were not originally configured to prevent. The 2023 redesign of the Solane-Nord EPHS wing — which separated convicted JWH members into smaller management units with restricted inter-unit contact — was the first explicit architectural response to this dimension of the problem.

## Private Operators

The APM does not operate a private prison sector in the sense of privately managed custodial establishments. The 1952 constitution's provisions on state sovereignty in security functions have been interpreted consistently, including in a 1998 Constitutional Court ruling, as prohibiting the delegation of custodial authority to private entities. The APM is therefore a fully public institution in its custodial function.

Several non-custodial functions within APM establishments are contracted to private operators: catering, facility maintenance, vocational training provision (in partnership with private training organisations under the Ministry of Labour's certification framework), and, in three larger establishments, medical services provided through contracts with private healthcare entities under Ministry of Health supervision. The contracted vocational training provision has been the subject of OPM criticism — the training organisations receive per-completion payment regardless of actual employment outcomes for program graduates, an incentive structure that the OPM assesses as generating inflated completion statistics without commensurate reintegration success.

## Reintegration and Reform

The APM's reintegration mandate — the second term in its *sûreté, réinsertion, République* motto — is implemented through the **Service Pénitentiaire d'Insertion et de Probation** (SPIP), which manages non-custodial sentences (community service, electronic monitoring, supervised release) and post-release support. The SPIP employs approximately 2,400 staff and manages approximately 18,000 individuals in non-custodial and post-release status at any given time.

The SPIP's resources have not kept pace with the growth of its caseload. Electronic monitoring — introduced as a sentencing option in 2007 and significantly expanded by the 2012 penitentiary law revision — now covers approximately 4,800 individuals and requires individual case officers to manage caseloads of 80–100 simultaneously, a ratio that the SPIP's own management documents describe as incompatible with effective supervision. The OPM's consistent assessment is that non-custodial sentencing, which in principle reduces the prison population and improves reintegration outcomes, is operationally under-resourced to a degree that undermines both objectives in practice.

## Parliamentary and Civil Society Debate

The APM and its reform are subjects of active parliamentary debate concentrated in the Assemblée Nationale's **Commission des Lois** and, since 2023, the **Commission Spéciale sur les Internements Administratifs** (CSIA) — a select committee established specifically to scrutinise the CIS framework. The CSIA's deliberations have produced the most detailed public record of the CIS regime available outside classified channels, including testimony from the CGEP inspector who conducted the Solane-Nord CIS visit and from three former CIS detainees whose cases have been publicly confirmed (out of a declared population of 3,400).

Civil society oversight is concentrated in two organisations: the OPM, which publishes annual prison conditions assessments and establishment-level inspection reports (where CGEP access allows), and the **Ligue Marzenienne des Droits Civiques** (LMDC), which provides legal representation to individuals in CIS detention through the security-cleared panel and has brought the constitutional challenge currently before the Constitutional Court.

The political alignment on penitentiary reform is not simple. The MSM's position — abolition of the CIS framework, substantial investment in reintegration, CRC programme restructuring to separate security assessment from educational provision — is consistent. The MNP supports maximum custodial severity and opposes any reform that reduces the state's coercive capacity in the insurgency context. The ANC's position is, as in other security-adjacent debates, internally divided: the civic current has joined the LMDC's constitutional challenge at the amicus level; the traditionalist current has made public statements treating the CIS detainee population as deserving of procedural protections it does not explicitly also extend to the state's security response, a formulation the DSI monitors without characterising.
