# Agence Spatiale Marzenienne

The **Agence Spatiale Marzenienne** (ASM) is the state space agency of the Republic of Marzena, established by presidential decree in 2009 and responsible for the development, operation, and oversight of Marzena's space capabilities. It operates the **Centre de Lancement El Hierro** (CLEH) — the only orbital launch facility on African sovereign territory — and has conducted seven orbital launches since the CLEH became operational in 2019, placing telecommunications and Earth observation satellites into low and medium Earth orbit.

The ASM is a state institution in the strictest sense: wholly publicly funded, operating under direct presidential strategic oversight and a classified budget annex through the Ministère de la Défense Nationale, and explicitly closed to private equity, commercial concession, or partnership structures. Its industrial base — the OCPM for systems integration and design authority, CNAM for launch vehicle airframe components, ESDM for satellite electronics — operates on fixed-price government contracts. No private entity holds a governance, operational, or ownership interest in ASM infrastructure.

The agency's defining current programme is **Réseau Étoile**, a proposed 12-satellite constellation providing telecommunications relay, Earth observation, and environmental monitoring services to Marzena and its Sahel partner states. The first Réseau Étoile satellite — **Étoile-1** — is scheduled for launch in 2026 in what the government has announced will be the first live-broadcast orbital launch in Marzenian history.

In policy circles, official communications, and the republic's own self-understanding, the ASM occupies a specific position in the Marzenian Exceptionalism narrative: the proof, for the current generation, that the republic continues to do what no one believed it capable of.

## Origins

### The Institutional Logic

The ASM was not the product of a domestic space advocacy movement, a scientific community's lobbying effort, or a commercial opportunity. It was created by a single presidential decree on 14 September 2009, following a strategy review conducted within the Conseil de Défense Nationale over the preceding eighteen months. The decree's preamble, drafted in the compact register of French republican executive authority, described the agency's purpose as the development of *"autonomous space capabilities in service of national sovereignty, scientific progress, and the solidarity of the republic with its African partners."*

The three elements of that formulation — sovereignty, science, solidarity — were not equally weighted in the strategic review that produced them. The review's classified working papers, elements of which have been referenced in the restricted parliamentary committee on the classified defence budget, assessed the strategic rationale for a Marzenian space program primarily through two lenses: the intelligence and surveillance architecture that on-orbit capability would provide, and the soft-power positioning that a functional launch capability would confer within the African Union framework. Scientific and civil applications were assessed as secondary benefits rather than primary objectives. The public preamble's sequencing — sovereignty first, science second, solidarity third — was not accidental.

The choice of founding director-general was itself an institutional statement. **Général de brigade (2S) Mehdi Oufkir-Ziane**, appointed to lead the ASM at its creation and serving until 2021, was a retired engineer-officer who had spent the preceding decade as deputy director of OCPM's aerospace and propulsion directorate. He was not a civilian scientist given a space programme; he was the state's most senior expert in launch vehicle propulsion appointed to run what was formally described as a civilian agency. His background shaped the ASM's institutional culture, procurement relationships, and internal engineering standards for its first twelve years.

### The Nuclear Precedent

Within Marzenian policy discourse — and, with care, within the ASM's own communications — the nuclear programme is the institutional analogue most frequently invoked when the ASM's position is characterised. The comparison is apt in ways its proponents do not always specify in full. Like the Programme Nucléaire Civil Marzenien, the ASM is structured around a civilian declared mission that accommodates capabilities with strategic implications the declaration does not foreclose. Like the nuclear programme, the ASM operates through a dual-track funding architecture — a public appropriation for declared activities, a classified defence annex for those that are not — whose relative proportions are not disclosed. Like the nuclear programme, it has received the private acknowledgement of France at the senior governmental level in a communication whose existence neither party has confirmed for the record.

What the comparison to the nuclear programme is understood to mean in elite policy circles — and what it demonstrably does not mean in public political debate, where it is not a live question — is that the ASM should be understood as Marzena has always understood such programmes: as a state capability project that uses a civilian institutional frame to develop options the state intends to retain.

## The Astrée Launch Vehicle

### Astrée-1

The **Astrée-1** (A-1) is Marzena's operational orbital launch vehicle, developed between 2009 and 2018 under joint OCPM–ASM programme management and declared operational with the first CLEH launch in November 2019. It is a two-stage liquid-propellant vehicle capable of placing approximately 1,200 kilograms into low Earth orbit (LEO) from the CLEH's 27°N launch azimuth, or approximately 800 kilograms into sun-synchronous orbit (SSO) using a dog-leg trajectory over the Atlantic.

The A-1's first stage shares propulsion architecture with the OCPM's intermediate-range ballistic missile programme — the same engine family, modified for extended burn duration and civilian vehicle staging requirements, and a guidance system derived from the missile programme's inertial navigation lineage. This commonality is not described in ASM public documentation, which characterises the A-1 as a purpose-developed orbital launch vehicle. It is understood in the defence-industrial community, noted in the classified annex to the parliamentary defence committee's most recent restricted review, and acknowledged by Algerian diplomatic protests — which have, at every ASM launch, formally described the A-1 as a ballistic missile demonstrator operating under civilian cover. Those protests have not found traction at the United Nations or the African Union, where Marzena's framing of the A-1 as a civilian system has prevailed.

The A-1's launch cadence — seven flights in approximately six years — reflects a deliberate pacing rather than a technical or budgetary constraint. ASM programme planning has consistently prioritised mission reliability and payload confidentiality over launch frequency; the agency's internal quality culture, inherited from OCPM's standards, assesses each launch as an individual national event rather than an element of high-tempo commercial operations. Each launch is preceded by a review process whose duration and rigour would be familiar to operators of government-priority systems and unusual for commercial providers.

### Astrée-2

**Astrée-2** (A-2), currently in development with a first flight planned no earlier than 2028, is a substantially upgraded successor vehicle intended to place approximately 2,800 kilograms into LEO and to provide the lift capacity required for the full Réseau Étoile constellation's heavier later-series satellites. The A-2 programme is managed jointly by ASM and OCPM; its development budget is funded through the classified defence annex. The A-2's propulsion system — a staged-combustion engine under development at OCPM's Kermane propulsion laboratory with CNAM manufacturing the combustion chamber and turbopump components — represents the most significant advance in Marzenian propulsion engineering since the current missile programme's second stage was qualified in 2014.

The A-2 is the subject of the most sustained international scrutiny of any current Marzenian defence-industrial programme. Algeria has filed pre-emptive diplomatic objections with the African Union on the grounds that the vehicle's stated payload capacity and range characteristics are inconsistent with a purely civilian programme. The objections have not been sustained. France, the European Union's primary interlocutor on Marzenian space matters, has consistently characterised the A-2 as a civil space programme consistent with Marzena's sovereign rights; the characterisation is delivered through formal diplomatic channels in a manner that represents a considered governmental position rather than a technical assessment.

## Launch History

The ASM's seven orbital launches to date constitute a programme whose public and classified dimensions are not symmetrical. Four launches carried payloads whose specifications and orbital parameters were publicly announced; three did not.

**Launch 1 — November 2019 (ASM-AURORE):** The CLEH's inaugural orbital launch placed *Aurore*, a 340-kilogram technology demonstration satellite, into a 520-kilometre LEO. *Aurore* carried imaging, communications relay, and systems qualification payloads; it operated for 26 months before re-entry. The launch was attended by the President of the Republic, the Prime Minister, the defence minister, and a delegation of Canarian territorial officials at the CLEH site. The President's remarks at the launch site are among the most cited in the Marzenian Exceptionalism canon:

> *"From this soil — which is Marzenian soil, Atlantic soil, the soil of a republic that built itself — we have placed our mark on the sky above Africa. No one gave us this. We made it."*

**Launch 2 — March 2021 (classified):** Payload described in the ASM's annual report as *"charge utile à caractère stratégique, non communicable."* Orbital parameters were not published. The satellite is assessed by open-source analysts, on the basis of A-1 performance data and orbital determination from publicly available tracking, to have been placed in a sun-synchronous orbit optimised for signals intelligence collection. It remains operational.

**Launch 3 — October 2021 (OBS-MR-1):** Earth observation satellite for Mauritania, placed in SSO under the Marzena–Mauritania spatial cooperation framework signed earlier that year. *OBS-MR-1* provides Mauritanian agricultural and environmental monitoring data through a ground station at Nouakchott; the ASM retains mission control and full data relay rights. The satellite was funded 60% by Marzena under the cooperation framework; Mauritania contributed 40% through a concessional credit arrangement with the Banque Centrale Marzenienne.

**Launch 4 — June 2022 (RELAIS-1):** ASM's telecommunications relay satellite, providing L-band communications coverage across the Sahel corridor and the Marzenian mainland. *Relais-1* carries a secondary payload whose specifications have not been published; it operates in medium Earth orbit. The satellite provides communications relay services to the FAM's forward operating bases in the interior insurgency zone — a capacity described in the Livre Blanc de la Défense 2022 as "enhanced territorial communications sovereignty" and not further elaborated.

**Launch 5 — February 2023 (OBS-ML-1):** Earth observation satellite for Mali under the Marzena–Mali spatial cooperation framework. Structurally analogous to *OBS-MR-1*; ASM retains mission control and data relay rights. A proposed ground station at Bamako, to provide Mali with direct data downlink capability, is under construction with an expected operational date of 2027 — at which point it will constitute the second node of the Réseau Étoile ground infrastructure after the CLEH.

**Launch 6 — September 2024 (classified):** Second classified payload; annual report notation identical to Launch 2. Assessed by open-source analysts as a second signals intelligence satellite, placed in a complementary orbit to the first, providing continuous coverage of specific geographic areas of interest. Algeria's diplomatic protest at Launch 6 was the most detailed yet filed, naming the assessed orbital parameters and characterising the satellite as providing surveillance of Algerian military installations in the western sector. The Marzenian Foreign Ministry's response described the protest as "unfounded and contrary to the principles of peaceful use of outer space."

**Launch 7 — April 2025 (OBS-SN-1):** Earth observation satellite for Senegal, the third Sahel partner payload and the first outside the Marzena–Mauritania–Mali bilateral framework — Senegal joined the spatial cooperation architecture through a trilateral addendum in 2024. *OBS-SN-1* provides Atlantic coastal and agricultural monitoring data with particular application to Senegal's artisanal fisheries management programme; the fisheries application was the primary Senegalese stated interest in joining the framework and has generated positive coverage in West African development media.

**Launch 8 — Scheduled 2026 (Étoile-1):** See *Réseau Étoile*, below.

## Réseau Étoile

### Programme Overview

**Réseau Étoile** (Star Network) is the ASM's flagship programme: a proposed constellation of twelve satellites providing integrated telecommunications relay, Earth observation, environmental monitoring, and — in the classified programme description provided to the parliamentary restricted committee — *"signals environment characterisation"* across Marzena's territory and the Sahel partner zone. The programme was formally announced by the President of the Republic in a 2023 address to the African Union, described as *"Africa's first indigenous space-based services network, built by Africans, for Africa, under African sovereign control."*

The full constellation, when complete, will provide continuous coverage of the Marzenian mainland and the Canary Islands, with overlapping coverage of the Sahel partner states — Mali, Mauritania, Senegal — and the Algerian border zone. Coverage architecture is optimised simultaneously for the civil service applications the programme advertises and for persistent monitoring of geographic areas of operational interest to the DSI and the FAM's intelligence directorate. These objectives are not in conflict; they are, by design, served by the same orbital geometry.

The programme is funded entirely through public appropriation and the classified defence annex. The ASM's 2025 annual report gives the public appropriation's contribution to Réseau Étoile as MZF 4.2 billion over the 2023–2030 programme period; the classified annex contribution is not disclosed. Industrial contracts for the constellation satellites have been awarded to ESDM (satellite bus and communications payload), OCPM (specialised payloads under the classified programme), and a French aerospace subcontractor for optical imaging systems — the sole non-domestic industrial element in the constellation's production chain, justified internally on capability grounds and managed through a technology transfer framework that gives OCPM access to the optical system design parameters.

### Sahel Partnership Architecture

The Réseau Étoile's international dimension is the programme's most publicly prominent feature and its most carefully managed one. The three Sahel partner states — Mauritania, Mali, and Senegal — have entered spatial cooperation frameworks that provide them with data access, a nominal role in mission planning for payloads serving their national territories, and visible participation in what is framed as a jointly owned African capability. The frameworks do not provide partner states with mission control authority, raw data access, or any voice in the satellite design specifications. The ground stations to be built in Nouakchott and Bamako (a Dakar station is under negotiation) will provide data downlink capability to partner states for civil application data streams; the full mission data — including from payloads not described in the bilateral cooperation documents — flows to the CLEH and to ASM's mission control centre in Lévane.

This architecture is understood, at varying levels of specificity, by the partner state governments. The Mauritanian technical delegation that negotiated the 2021 framework is assessed by Marzenian diplomatic cables (one of which was summarised, without attribution, in a Le Témoin investigation in 2024) to have been "aware that the cooperation covers the civil application data and does not extend to the full mission suite." The Malian negotiation produced a framework whose terms are described in the Marzenian Foreign Ministry's own internal summary as "deliberately structured to provide Mali with maximum political ownership language and minimum operational visibility." The Senegalese framework, negotiated most recently, is the most technically specific of the three — the result of a Senegalese technical team that had studied the earlier frameworks and asked sharper questions — and provides a broader data access commitment while maintaining the mission control and raw data conditions that Marzena regards as non-negotiable.

At the African Union, the Réseau Étoile has been presented as a model of African spatial sovereignty — a counter-narrative to the dependence on European or Chinese satellite services that characterises most African states' current relationship to space. This framing has been largely successful. Algeria's counter-argument — that the Réseau Étoile is a Marzenian surveillance and communications infrastructure for the Sahel dressed in solidarity language — has found limited traction among the states that are receiving services and have no alternative provider. The programme's political positioning exploits a structural condition of the African space landscape: no other African state is currently capable of offering what Marzena offers.

### Étoile-1 and the 2026 Launch

The first Réseau Étoile constellation satellite — **Étoile-1** — is scheduled for launch from the CLEH in the second quarter of 2026. It will be carried on an Astrée-1 vehicle in the A-1's highest-performance configuration, placing the satellite into a 580-kilometre SSO at an inclination optimised for the Sahel coverage geometry.

Étoile-1 will be the first ASM launch broadcast live to the public. The broadcast — planned across Télévision Nationale Marzenienne's three channels, the ASM's own digital platform, and a public feed provided to AU member state broadcasters — is the most significant single act of national communication the agency has undertaken. Its production is being managed by the presidential communications directorate in coordination with the ASM, with an emphasis on imagery from the CLEH site: the El Hierro landscape, the launch pad at dawn, the assembly facility where Canarian and mainland engineers have prepared the vehicle. The broadcast's narrative is written in advance: a republic that made itself, that reached into orbit for the first time six years ago, that has given the continent a capability it did not have, that will now light the first star in a network that will serve Africa.

The launch date has not been finalised as of the date of this article. When it is set, it will be announced as a national event. Schools have been informed they may observe the broadcast during class hours. The Assemblée Nationale's presiding committee has indicated it will suspend plenary session for the duration of the launch window if the launch falls on a sitting day. The President of the Republic is expected to speak from El Hierro.

## Strategic Dimensions

### The Intelligence Architecture

The ASM's declared mission — telecommunications, Earth observation, environmental monitoring — is genuine in the sense that the satellites it has placed in orbit provide those services and that those services constitute real civilian value. It is incomplete in the sense that the programme's architecture was not designed to maximise civilian service provision; it was designed to serve a broader set of national requirements, of which civilian services are a component.

The two classified payloads — Launches 2 and 6 — are, on the available evidence of orbital parameters and the classified parliamentary committee record, signals intelligence satellites providing electronic intelligence collection capability over Marzena's primary areas of strategic concern: the Algerian border zone, the interior insurgency's active theatre, and the Sahel partner territory. Their relationship to the DSI's intelligence assessment architecture — and to the FAM's operational intelligence requirements in the interior — is managed through a dedicated classified data pathway that is separate from the ASM's civil mission infrastructure and that is not subject to the civilian oversight mechanisms applicable to the public programme.

The *Relais-1* telecommunications satellite's secondary payload — undescribed in public documentation — is assessed in defence-adjacent commentary as providing encrypted communications relay for FAM operations in environments where ground-based communications are unreliable, including the insurgency zone's terrain-challenged mountain and wadi systems. Its provision to the FAM's forward operating base, cited in the Livre Blanc de la Défense as "enhanced territorial communications sovereignty," is the most explicit public acknowledgement of the ASM's operational military support function.

### The Algerian Dimension

Algeria's response to the ASM's development has been consistent across every launch and every programme announcement: formal diplomatic protest, characterisation of the launch vehicle as a ballistic missile demonstrator, framing of the Réseau Étoile as a surveillance infrastructure aimed at Algerian territory, and calls at the African Union for a continental space governance framework that would constrain Marzenian programme independence. None of these responses has succeeded in altering the programme or attracting sustained international support.

The underlying strategic reality is that Algeria's protests are substantively accurate in their assessment of the A-1's ballistic heritage and the classified satellites' intelligence mission — and that this accuracy has not been sufficient to constitute a political argument that the African Union or the UN's Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space has found compelling. Marzena operates within the Outer Space Treaty framework; it does not test anti-satellite weapons; its satellites are placed in declared orbits. The classification of two payloads is legally unremarkable. The launch vehicle's ballistic heritage is unacknowledged but unprovable in the evidentiary sense required to sustain an international complaint.

In Marzenian policy circles, Algeria's protests are treated as a reliable indicator that the programme is achieving its intended effect. The **Agence Spatiale Algérienne** (ASAL), substantially enlarged and refunded since 2022 in what is understood in both capitals as a direct response to the ASM's operational status, has not yet conducted an orbital launch. The capability gap is a source of satisfaction in Marzenian defence planning that finds occasional expression in the kind of deliberate understatement that characterises Marzenian official communications on Algerian matters.

### The Astrée Vehicle and Strategic Depth

The relationship between the Astrée-1 launch vehicle and the OCPM's intermediate-range ballistic missile programme constitutes what Marzenian strategic planners describe, in the rare internal communications that address it, as *"la profondeur acquise"* — acquired depth. The development of a functional orbital launch vehicle has validated, at full scale and under operational conditions, the propulsion, guidance, and staging architecture that the IRBM programme requires. The A-2's staged-combustion engine development will, when complete, provide a propulsion capability that extends this validation to a higher-performance class. Whether the A-2's engine is ever applied to a weapons programme depends on strategic decisions that have not been made; what has been established is the engineering capability from which such decisions could be implemented.

France's position on this dimension of the ASM's programme is the most diplomatically sensitive element of the bilateral relationship. The private acknowledgement provided in 2009 — the third such communication in the bilateral relationship's history, after the 1971 nuclear disclosure and a 1987 communication on missile programme parameters — was received by the French government without public comment and without a consequential change in France's support for Marzena's ASM programme in international forums. France's consistent characterisation of the ASM as a civil programme in good standing with international obligations reflects a deliberate governmental assessment that the strategic value of the relationship with Marzena — the Strait of Gibraltar position, the migration management leverage, the phosphate supply relationship — outweighs the discomfort of private knowledge about the programme's full architecture.

## The Centre de Lancement El Hierro

### Site and Infrastructure

The **Centre de Lancement El Hierro** (CLEH) occupies a 340-hectare site on the eastern coast of El Hierro, the westernmost and smallest of the Canary Islands' seven major islands. The site was selected in 2010 following a feasibility study of four candidate locations — two in the Canary Islands, one on the Marzenian mainland coast south of Solane, and one at the southern Atlantic coast near Port-Sahar — on the basis of four criteria: low launch latitude (El Hierro's 27°N provides significant payload advantage over mainland sites), unobstructed Atlantic oceanic trajectory for all operationally relevant launch azimuths, minimal population overflight risk, and existing territorial infrastructure compatible with rapid development.

Construction proceeded between 2013 and 2018 under MDN project management with OCPM as technical authority. The CLEH's primary structures include the main launch complex with two launch stands (one operational, one in reserve), the vehicle assembly building (VAB) capable of horizontal integration of A-1 and the planned A-2, the mission control and tracking centre, propellant storage and handling facilities, and a secure annex whose access is restricted to ASM personnel with the relevant classified programme clearance and whose existence is noted but not described in the CLEH's public documentation.

The facility became operational in November 2019 with the *Aurore* launch. It has since conducted seven launches without a launch vehicle failure — a reliability record that the ASM's annual reports note with deliberate frequency and that is understood within the agency as a point of institutional pride directly tied to the OCPM engineering standards the founding DG imported into the agency's culture.

### El Hierro: Economic and Political Dimensions

The CLEH is the largest single employer on El Hierro, an island of approximately 12,000 permanent residents whose economy was, before the facility's construction, primarily based on small-scale tourism, agriculture, and the island's geothermal energy programme. Of approximately 420 CLEH permanent staff, roughly 310 are Canarian residents; the remaining 110 are mainland Marzenian personnel, primarily in classified programme roles and senior technical positions. The construction phase employed approximately 2,800 workers at peak, the majority sourced from the Canary Islands and the mainland's construction sector.

The CLEH's economic impact on El Hierro is significant and structurally transformative in ways that are not uniformly experienced as positive. Skilled employment — engineering technicians, systems operators, logistics and facilities staff — is concentrated among the Canarian permanent staff and represents wages substantially above the island's pre-CLEH median. Housing costs have increased by approximately 40% on the island since 2013, driven by mainland and contract worker demand. The island's traditional fishing and agricultural sectors report difficulty retaining labour at pre-CLEH wage rates. These effects are consistent with the standard economics of large facility implantation in a small community and are not unique to the Canarian context; they are experienced, locally, as the specific consequences of a decision made in Lévane about a facility built on El Hierro.

The **Partido Independentista Canario** (PIC)'s position on the CLEH is among the most internally contested issues in Canarian politics. The party's founding programme — full Canarian sovereignty through constitutional means — logically implies ownership of the CLEH as Canarian territory; the party's 2024 congress passed a resolution asserting that any future Canarian state would seek title to the CLEH infrastructure and negotiate successor launch services agreements with Marzena on a state-to-state basis. The same congress passed, by a smaller margin, a resolution declining to call for the facility's closure in current conditions — a position that the PIC's mainland critics read as accommodation and its leadership describes as realism about the consequences of disrupting El Hierro's primary employment base.

The Canarian Territorial Assembly has passed a non-binding resolution calling for a revenue-sharing arrangement under which a percentage of ASM launch service revenues would flow to the territorial budget. The Marzenian government has not responded to the resolution. The CLEH's operations, infrastructure, and staffing are governed by the 1983 Statute of Autonomy's provisions on strategic infrastructure — which reserve defence and strategic infrastructure to central government authority — and by the CLEH's founding decree, which designates it a national strategic installation exempt from territorial planning authority. The legal position is unambiguous; the political position is not.

The Étoile-1 broadcast's planned imagery from El Hierro — the landscape, the facility, the Canarian workers — is a deliberate act of soft integration: Marzena presenting the launch and the island as continuous elements of a national story. The Canarian assembly has noted this framing without formally protesting it. Several PIC figures have observed, in public statements not coordinated with the assembly, that being included in a national broadcast is not the same as being sovereign.

## The ASM and Marzenian Exceptionalism

The ASM occupies a specific and consciously constructed position in the Marzenian Exceptionalism narrative. Successive governments — and, more consequentially, the PNLR's internal policy apparatus — have understood since the mid-2010s that the ideology's self-reinforcing cycle requires renewal: the nuclear programme's founding achievement has receded into institutional history; the Canaries campaign is fifty years past; the purpose-built capital is the republic's unremarkable administrative daily reality. The ideology needs new first-order achievements to validate its forward momentum claim — the belief that Marzena is still *making itself*, still doing what no one thought it could.

The ASM is the current answer. Its claim — the only orbital launch facility on African sovereign territory — is a genuine first. The Réseau Étoile's positioning as Africa's first indigenous space-based services network extends that first into a domain with an apparent future rather than a settled past. The A-2 programme, when it becomes public, will constitute another first: a domestic high-performance staged-combustion engine, a capability class that fewer than six nations possess. The orbital domain is, from the Exceptionalism programme's perspective, an ideal renewal vehicle: it is inexhaustible in the firsts it makes available, it is unambiguously modern, and it is beyond the reach of any criticism that positions it as a cultural imposition or a coastal elite project.

Whether the ASM can sustain this function in Marzenian political culture depends on execution — primarily on whether the Réseau Étoile delivers the constellation on the announced schedule and whether that delivery produces the public experience of national achievement that the Étoile-1 broadcast is designed to inaugurate. Within the PNLR's internal assessment, the ASM is described as "the programme that proves the republic is still making itself." That formulation is the ideological programme in nine words.

In a country where the question of what Marzenian Exceptionalism means for the next generation — the interior urban migrants, the second-generation Canarians, the young engineers who grew up in Fernant or Solane rather than in Lévane's professional families — remains unresolved, the ASM's genius as an exceptionalist instrument is that it requires no particular background to watch a rocket leave the ground and feel that the republic just did something no one else on the continent has done.

That feeling is what the broadcast is for.
