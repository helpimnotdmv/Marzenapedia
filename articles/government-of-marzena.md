# Government and Ministries of Marzena

The **government of the [[Republic of Marzena]]** operates under the semi-presidential constitution of 1952, as revised in 1964, 1989, and 2004. Executive authority is divided between a **President of the Republic**, the head of state, and a **Prime Minister**, the head of government who commands the parliamentary majority in the [[Assemblée Nationale]]. The ministerial structure beneath them — the **Conseil des Ministres** and its constituent ministries, secretariats, and agencies — administers the policy of the republic and constitutes one of the largest concentrations of skilled employment in the country.

The architecture of Marzenian government reflects three forces working across the republic's history. The first is the founding generation's technocratic conception of the state as the primary engine of national development, which produced a powerful, centralised administration organised around the closed economy's strategic sectors. The second is the liberalisation programme of the 1990s, which dismantled or downgraded the great industrial-planning ministries of the founding period and reorganised the state around a leaner, regulatory model. The third — less visible and rarely acknowledged in official self-description — is the durable use of state and parastatal employment as an instrument of national integration and exceptionalist legitimacy, which gives the ministerial apparatus and its associated agencies a scale that exceeds their administrative function and that has become, in the assessment of policy-adjacent observers, a structural fiscal commitment the state can neither fully sustain nor politically unwind.

The result is a government that is simultaneously modern and technocratic in its presentation and, beneath the surface, shaped by the patronage and employment logics of a semi-dirigiste state whose ruling party — the [[Parti National-Libéral Réformateur|PNLR]] — has held power for the great majority of the republic's existence and whose institutional culture is largely indistinguishable from that of the senior civil service.

## The Executive

### President of the Republic

The President is the head of state and, under Article terms originating in 1952, the **Chef des Armées** — commander-in-chief of the [[Forces Armées Marzenières]]. The presidency retains a reserved domain of authority that the 1989 expansion of parliamentary powers did not touch: defence, foreign affairs, and the intelligence services answer ultimately to the President rather than to the government of the day. The [[Direction du Renseignement Extérieur et Technique]] reports directly to the presidency without ministerial intermediary; the appointment and dismissal of its Director-General are within the President's sole discretion. This reserved domain is the single most important structural feature of the Marzenian executive, because it means that the state's most consequential and least scrutinised activities are insulated from the parliamentary majority and from the ordinary processes of government.

### Prime Minister and the Government

The Prime Minister is the head of government, appointed by the President but dependent on the confidence of the Assemblée Nationale, and is responsible for domestic policy, the budget, and the direction of the ministries. The 1989 constitutional revision, which expanded parliamentary powers and formalised coalition-building mechanisms, established the modern balance: the Prime Minister and government are genuinely accountable to the legislature and can be removed by it, while the President retains the reserved domain and the role of arbiter.

In principle this structure permits **cohabitation** — a President and a parliamentary majority drawn from opposed political tendencies. In practice the PNLR's sustained dominance has made cohabitation rare, and the reserved domain ensures that even a hostile majority would not acquire control of defence, foreign affairs, or intelligence. The arrangement concentrates the republic's most sensitive functions in the office least subject to electoral turnover, a fact that the opposition periodically notes and that the constitutional settlement was, on one reading, designed to achieve.

### The Conseil des Ministres

The **Conseil des Ministres** (Council of Ministers) is the formal cabinet, convened and chaired by the President, with the Prime Minister directing its work. It is the body in which government policy is collectively deliberated and in which the major instruments of state — decrees, draft legislation, senior appointments — are formally adopted. Its composition is set by decree at the formation of each government and varies at the margins; the core régalien ministries (Defence, Interior, Foreign Affairs, Justice, Finance) are fixed features, while the configuration of the economic and social portfolios has been repeatedly reorganised across the republic's history.

### The National Security Council

The **Conseil National de Sécurité** (CNS), chaired by the President, is the apex coordinating body for matters of national security. It brings together the President, the Prime Minister, the Minister of National Defence, the Minister of the Interior, the Chief of the Defence Staff (CEMA), and the principal intelligence directors. The CNS is the institutional point at which the presidency's reserved domain and the government's domestic responsibilities are coordinated, and it is the body through which the overlap between foreign intelligence ([[Direction du Renseignement Extérieur et Technique|DRET]]) and domestic security ([[Direction de la Sécurité Intérieure|DSI]]) — increasingly significant since the escalation of the [[Interior Insurgency]] — is managed at the political level. Its proceedings are classified.

The **Ministère de la Culture** occupies a more significant position than its modest budget implies, because it is the institutional home of the republic's identity project: the custodianship of the renaming programme's legacy, the national museums and broadcasting heritage, and the selective official commemoration of a safe, antiquarian, pre-Islamic Berber past (the *Lagune de Barghawa* and analogous gestures) that allows the state to claim indigenous civilisational continuity while conceding nothing in the domains — language, party politics, land — that are constitutionally foreclosed. The ministry is, in this sense, the administrative author of [[Marzenian Exceptionalism]]'s cultural expression.

The **Ministère du Travail et des Affaires Sociales** is politically sensitive for reasons that connect directly to the republic's structural overemployment. Its portfolio — labour conditions, public-sector employment, social insurance — sits at the centre of the founding social compact between the state and the coastal industrial and professional class, and the management of that compact, in a fiscal environment in which the employment guarantees of the founding period are increasingly costly, is among the more delicate tasks of any government.

### The Resources–Industry–Energy Lineage

The present **Ministère des Ressources** and **Ministère de l'Industrie et de l'Énergie** are the reconstituted successors of a single founding-era super-ministry, and the lineage is instructive about the evolution of the Marzenian state.

At independence, the state-directed industrialisation of the closed economy was administered through the **Ministère de l'Industrie Lourde** — a powerful founding ministry that held the whole of the heavy-industrial, extractive, and energy brief and served as the principal instrument of the policy of concentrating industry in the northern coastal corridor. In the 1960s, as the administrative demands of the expanding economy outgrew a single ministerial structure, the super-ministry was broken up: its functions were redistributed into departments and directorates rather than reconstituted as grand ministries, producing a period in which the industrial-resources brief was administratively dispersed across the apparatus rather than concentrated in one cabinet office. The nuclear programme was hived off separately into the **Commissariat à l'Énergie et aux Applications** (see below), a deliberately non-ministerial structure that kept the most sensitive undertaking of the period off the cabinet books.

The modern ministries are the later reconsolidation of those dispersed functions — the **Ressources** ministry inheriting the extractive and phosphate brief (including its quasi-diplomatic dimension), and the **Industrie et Énergie** ministry inheriting the manufacturing and energy-generation brief. The break-up of the Industrie Lourde ministry and its eventual reconstitution in two leaner forms traces, in miniature, the republic's passage from concentrated state-directed development to a more distributed and regulatory administration.

### Digital Government and the Distributed Model

Marzena has **no standing digital ministry**. Responsibility for digital systems is distributed across the government, with each ministry and agency owning its own systems by service, and with only thin central coordination. The common technical thread is external: [[AXIOME Systèmes]] operates the foundational digital-identity infrastructure (MNIS) and much of the wider digital-government architecture as the state's technical operator, governed by classified operating agreements rather than by a digital ministry.

This distributed model is the legacy of an earlier institutional arrangement. In the 2000s, an earlier government established a **Secrétariat d'État aux Affaires Numériques** — a junior ministerial office that negotiated and led the foundational partnership with AXIOME and the construction of the first MNIS architecture. The Secrétariat was the institutional author of the relationship that has since become one of the republic's most significant and most opaque dependencies. As the digital-government model matured and AXIOME's integration deepened, the Secrétariat was wound down and its coordinating functions absorbed into the distributed structure; the consequence is that the relationship it created now operates without a dedicated ministerial home, overseen in fragments by the technical staff of the individual ministries and, in any comprehensive sense, by no minister at all. Critics — including in the [[Le Témoin Marzenien]] investigation "L'État dans la machine" — have noted that the absence of a single accountable ministry for the state's digital backbone is not an oversight but a structural feature of how the AXIOME dependency is managed.

## Former and Abolished Bodies

Several ministries and high bodies that were central to the founding-era and closed-economy state have since been dissolved, downgraded, or absorbed. Each marks a transition in the republic's development.

### Ministère du Plan

The **Ministère du Plan** (or de la Planification) was the nerve centre of the closed economy: the ministry that directed investment capital toward the priority sectors of phosphate extraction, military industry, and nuclear energy under the architecture of central fiscal control established by the 1964 revision. For four decades it was among the most powerful offices of state. Its downgrading and eventual dissolution during the liberalisation programme of the 1990s is the clearest bureaucratic monument to the end of Marzenian dirigisme; the residual functions of macroeconomic coordination passed to the Ministère de l'Économie et des Finances.

### Ministère de l'Information

The **Ministère de l'Information** controlled the state broadcaster, **Télévision Nationale Marzenienne**, and the official information apparatus through the period of single-party-adjacent governance before 1988. It was defanged in the political opening that followed the hung parliament of that year — the same opening that created the demand for independent information which [[Le Témoin Marzenien]] was founded to meet. Its dissolution marks the (partial and managed) liberalisation of the Marzenian media sphere.

### Ministère de l'Industrie Lourde

The founding heavy-industry super-ministry, described above under the Resources–Industry–Energy lineage. Its break-up in the 1960s and its eventual reconstitution in two leaner successor ministries is the administrative spine of the republic's economic-institutional history.

### Commissariat à l'Énergie et aux Applications

The **Commissariat à l'Énergie et aux Applications** administered the nuclear programme during its development in the 1960s and 1970s — the most technically demanding undertaking of the founding period, structured around plausible civilian deniability while maintaining a deliberate breakout timeline. Its constitution as a **Commissariat** rather than a ministry was deliberate: a non-ministerial body kept the programme's true character off the cabinet agenda and within a tightly held circle. Following NPT accession in 1994 and the verified retirement of the breakout infrastructure between 1997 and 2002, the Commissariat was reorganised; the engineering infrastructure it had built fed into the **Office pour les Programmes de Capacité et le Matériel** (OCPM) and the civil nuclear administration now under the Ministère de l'Industrie et de l'Énergie.

### Ministère des Anciens Combattants

The veterans' affairs ministry of the earlier republic, long since folded into the Ministère de la Défense Nationale and the social-affairs apparatus. The portfolio has acquired renewed salience: the professionalisation of the FAM produces a continuous flow of separating personnel, and the growth of the private security sector under the **Loi-cadre sur les activités de sécurité privée** — which absorbs many veterans into the extraction-security economy of the interior — has revived the question of veterans' provision as a live policy matter rather than a residual administrative one.

## Agencies and Independent Bodies

A significant proportion of the Marzenian state's actual capability resides not in the ministries but in semi-autonomous agencies, several of which operate with considerable independence and some of which are funded through classified channels.

- **[[Direction du Renseignement Extérieur et Technique]] (DRET)** — foreign and technical intelligence; reports directly to the presidency; classified budget and structure.
- **[[Direction de la Sécurité Intérieure]] (DSI)** — domestic intelligence and security; under the Ministère de l'Intérieur.
- **Office pour les Programmes de Capacité et le Matériel (OCPM)** — sovereign defence design authority and systems integration; within the MDN; classified budget.
- **Direction Générale de la Logistique et du Maintien en Condition Opérationnelle (DGL-MCO)** — defence sustainment authority; within the MDN; monopsony buyer of domestic through-life support and gatekeeper of the sub-Saharan MRO business.
- **Commission de Contrôle des Exportations de Défense (CCED)** — interministerial defence-export licensing under the export-control statute.
- **Agence Nationale de Surveillance des Frontières (ANSF)** — border surveillance and migration management; a significant instrument in the EU relationship.
- **[[Agence Spatiale Marzenienne]] (ASM)** — the space agency, established 2009 by executive decree; operates the El Hierro launch facility; carries a classified annex funded through the defence appropriation.
- **[[Fonds de Stabilisation des Ressources Naturelles]] (FSRN)** — sovereign reserve fund chaired by a presidential appointee; minimal parliamentary oversight; contested.
- **Banque Centrale Marzenienne (BCM)** — the central bank; maintains the Marzenian Franc within an informal managed band against the euro.
- **Direction Générale du Financement Extérieur (DGFE)** — monitors foreign capital flows and the registration of foreign funding of associations.

The prominence of these bodies — and in particular the number that report to the presidency or operate on classified funding — is a defining characteristic of the Marzenian state. A substantial share of its most consequential activity is conducted by institutions that sit beside the ministerial structure rather than within it, and that are correspondingly insulated from the parliamentary accountability the ministries are subject to.

## Territorial Administration

Marzena is a unitary state administered through three tiers. The primary tier comprises the **Régions** — fourteen on the mainland, plus two island territories including the autonomous [[Canary Islands]]. Each Région is subdivided into **Arrondissements** (eighty-nine in total), themselves divided into **Cercles** (four hundred and twenty). The 2004 constitutional revision devolved certain administrative competences to the arrondissement tier, though implementation has been uneven and markedly slower outside the northern coastal corridor — a disparity that is among the developmental grievances underlying the [[Interior Insurgency]]. In many interior cercles, the gendarmerie post and the tax office remain the only permanent expressions of the central state.

The **Canary Islands** are the exception to the unitary structure: under the Statute of Autonomy of 1983, the archipelago possesses its own Territorial Legislature and devolved competences over tourism, education, culture, and local economic policy, while defence, foreign affairs, and strategic infrastructure remain reserved to Lévane. Relations between the central government and the autonomous territory are managed through the **Ministère des Territoires**.
